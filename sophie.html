Chapter 1: My earliest memory?

My earliest memory? I was in my father’s arms. I suspect I was around three. We were in my parent’s bedroom. They were going out because she was dressed in a blue fluttery thing and smelled lemony. My mother was on the floor screaming, railing about not finding a shoe. She tended to be hysterical. After all, if one shoe was lost, the other had to be pretty close. I watched her crouch and crawl along the floor.

Beneath the bed was a shoe, but not the shoe she was looking for. She threw it into the air; it hit a lamp and crashed to the floor. The lightbulb popped, and shards of glass flew everywhere. My father had to call the cleaning lady, who spent the rest of the day washing and vacuuming every surface. My mother had been a ballet dancer. Just the thought of the slimmest slice into her “livelihood” would mean “unbearable pain.” By the way, her last performance was before I was born. I guess when people have unmaterialized, unrealized, and stupid dreams, it gives them carte blanche to speak endlessly of regrets and lost opportunities. Whine, Whine. She could have become a contender, but then she got pregnant with me. What was I supposed to do? Force birth control pills down her throat?

Anyway, they never went out, and later that evening, I found the shoe. It was remarkably buried in the bathroom trash basket. I was very proud of myself and took it to her as she was lying on the couch—excuse me, the divan—after a couple of whiskeys. Do you see where this is going? No, of course not. No one ever did. She told me it was the original shoe and to put it back in the bathroom, which I did. Years later, I realized she must have known where I found the shoe because she told me where to return it: in the bathroom basket. I had never told her where I had found it. I simply brought it to her. Of course, it could have been the first shoe I had seen, but why would she have put it in the bathroom bin under the sink beneath a pile of tissues? The point was my mother was an alcoholic, and I lived in a household where stories made little sense, and everyone lied. I learned from the best.

Where was my father? Do you mean during the incident? Good question. Let me think... He held me for a while, then put me down. While the cleaning lady was there, he hovered, and I went back to playing with my dolls. I enjoyed pretending they had broken arms or legs. That’s why I had gone into the bathroom in the first place. I could often find used tissues from my mother’s makeup to wrap up their legs or arms.

Huh? No, I wasn’t being mean. I was showing how brave the little girls were. They jumped off railings, dressers, and even the second-floor window. They were preteen superheroes. Upon landing, they broke a body part but didn’t cry. They were heroic. I bandaged them up, and the other dolls treated them with respect and awe. In case you were wondering, I never dismembered any of my dolls, sliced them, or put them in the microwave. I like bandaging them up with tissue and tape. When they were better, I took the bandages off, and they fully recovered. Well, until the next time. Looking back on it, I think I was a fairly sensitive child.

Chapter 2: Did we ever get along?

Did we ever get along? I guess. She enjoyed picking out my clothes when I started school. I certainly had her attention back then. Whatever she bought, I wore. I sensed that if I complied with her fashion sense, she’d leave me alone. She took a ton of pictures of me during my kindergarten year. And to be honest, I looked pretty darn good: leggings, tunics, prairie dresses, farmers’ overalls, cargo shorts, pleated skirts, cable sweaters, blazers, headbands, bracelets, sunglasses, T-shirts, Mary Janes, work, rain, and cowboy boots and a ton of multi-colored, multi-patterned tights and socks—all mixed and matched. I particularly remember the cowboy boots. I had to make sure my socks didn’t go above the boot line. She wanted me to have only my bare legs or tights above the boot. Otherwise, she said it looked “too busy.” It must have made an impression because I never wore cowboy boots after that year.

The school was a couple of blocks away. She told me to hold her left hand, never her right. Her right hand was for waving to people on the sidewalk or in cars. She must have been popular in the neighborhood because cars would beep, and people, most of the male variety, would call out, “Hey, Mrs. Jamison.” She’d yell back, “Bonjour.” I liked the walk to school because she seemed happy and smiled a lot. Far more than she ever did at home.

What else did she seem to like? Do you mean besides drinking? Shopping for hours and spending money, of course. Once, she bought me a set of those Russian Nesting dolls. The first few were cute. I mean, what’s not to love about little fat ladies hidden inside each other? But before long, we had about twenty sets in a curio cabinet, and it got weird.

One night, after a few too many, she put them on the kitchen table and called me from my bedroom. I was still pretty young, maybe six.

“Sophie, I need to show you something.”

She had three in a row, different colors, black, yellow, and pink.

“Which one do you like?”

I picked the black one because I thought that wasn’t one of her favorites. She pulled it from the line and placed it in front of me. Then she talked out loud.

“Okay, I have to choose. Yellow or pink? I like yellow because it’s sunny and bright, but I don’t feel sunny or bright. The pink is pretty but a little too girly.”

I didn’t know what to do. Offer her mine or keep quiet? Either could have consequences. Racine was unpredictable.

“Sophie, what do you think?”

Suddenly I was in the bull’s eye. I thought fast. “You like bananas more than strawberries.”

She thought for a moment. “That’s true. But there’s more to it than just the colors, right?”

I must have shrugged, trying to grasp what she was saying or implying or rambling on about. I was utterly lost.

“Look into their eyes.” She picked up the yellow one. “What do these eyes say to you?”

At first, all the eyes looked the same as the others, but when I compared the yellow and pink dolls, the differences were noticeable. The pink one had more eyelashes, and the yellow only had a couple. It made her look sad, forgotten. I showed my mother what I meant, and she agreed with me. She then picked the yellow one and put it in front of her.

“Sophie, these are hand-painted and rare. Probably over one hundred years old. It makes them very special, but what’s most remarkable is that whoever painted them is with us in this very room.”

Now here’s the thing: when you’re young and impressionable, this observation seems plausible. I looked at my black doll. Its lips were so red; they looked bloody. I remember pushing it away and saying I didn’t like it.

“No! There’s a lesson here. You picked that doll, and now you must learn from it.” She shoved it in my face. “What is the doll saying to you?”

I started to cry. If this was a game, it was going badly. But she wouldn’t give up and demanded to know what it said to me.

I was beside myself and yelled. “I’m a witch, and I’m evil.”

She sat back in the chair. “Just as I thought.”

And so went an unforgettable evening with mother.

Chapter 3: Tell me about the Russian dolls

Tell me about the Russian dolls? Well, that's a strange place to start, but sure. My mother had this thing about shopping. Not just normal shopping, obsessive, compulsive, fill-the-void-with-stuff shopping. Everything had to be perfect: our clothes, the furniture, the artwork, even the damn hand soap. People always commented on how "put together" our house was, which made her beam. What they didn't see was the mountain of credit card debt that would sometimes reduce her to tears when my father was out of town.

The Russian dolls started innocently enough. We were at an antique market when I was about six. She spotted this set of nesting dolls, matryoshkas, she called them, and became completely entranced. They were hand-painted, the woman told her, from some remote village outside Moscow. The outermost doll was a plump woman with rosy cheeks wearing a red and gold scarf, her eyes slightly downcast as if she were hiding something. Inside her were six more dolls, each smaller but with subtle differences in their expressions.

"Sophie, look at the craftsmanship," she whispered, holding the largest doll close to my face. I remember the smell of dust and old wood. "Each one is an individual, but they belong together. A family inside a family."

My father rolled his eyes but paid the exorbitant price she'd already negotiated down somewhat. He knew better than to fight her when she got that look, the one where her eyes widened and her voice got higher, like she'd discovered the secret to happiness.

One set wasn't enough, of course. Within six months, we had five different sets. By the time I was seven, there were fifteen. Eventually, there was an entire curio cabinet in our living room devoted to these wooden families, staring out with their painted smiles and vacant eyes. Sometimes when I was alone, I'd open the glass doors and arrange them differently, mixing families together or standing them in rows like a wooden army.

My mother would periodically take them all out, dust them, and reorganize them. It was one of the few household tasks she did herself instead of leaving for our cleaning lady, Maria. She'd handle each doll with reverence, occasionally showing me a particularly interesting one. "Look at this one, Sophie. See how the artist painted tiny tears in her eyes? You can barely see them unless you know to look."

I never saw the tears she talked about, but I nodded anyway.

The drinking and the dolls seemed to go together. She'd pour herself a glass of something, usually whiskey, sometimes vodka, and sit in front of the cabinet, staring at her collection. Sometimes she'd talk to them. Sometimes to me. Sometimes to no one at all.

The night of the kitchen table incident happened when I was six and a half. It was past midnight, and I should have been asleep. I'd gotten up to get water and found her sitting at the kitchen table with three sets of dolls arranged in front of her. An empty whiskey glass sat nearby, and she was working on a second. The overhead light was off, but the small lamp above the stove cast everything in a yellow glow.

"Sophie," she said, not looking up. "Perfect timing. Come sit with me."

I knew better than to refuse. So I climbed into the chair across from her, my bare feet dangling above the floor. The house was quiet except for the hum of the refrigerator.

She had arranged three dolls in a row, black, yellow, and pink, just like I told you before. Each was the outermost doll of a different set. The black one was my least favorite. It had always unsettled me with its blood-red lips and eyes that seemed to follow you.

"Sophie, I need to show you something important," she said, her words slightly stretched, the way they always were after her second drink.

She lined them up and asked which one I liked best. I picked the black one, thinking she would want the others for herself. Even at that age, I was calculating, trying to anticipate what would make her happy, what would prevent a scene.

She placed the black doll in front of me and began her strange monologue, debating between the yellow and pink. "Yellow is sunny," she mused, "but I don't feel sunny. Pink is pretty but maybe too girly."

The way she spoke, it wasn't to me. It was like I was watching her perform in a play where she was both the actor and the audience. I sat frozen, waiting for my cue.

"Sophie, what do you think?" she finally asked, turning those glassy eyes toward me.

My heart raced. I knew this wasn't a simple question. With my mother, questions were tests, and the right answer was always changing. I tried to think like her, what would she want me to say?

"You like bananas more than strawberries," I offered, connecting the colors to something concrete, something I knew about her.

This seemed to please her. "That's true," she said, nodding slowly. "But there's more to it than just the colors, right?"

I shrugged, having exhausted my six-year-old insight.

"Look into their eyes," she instructed, picking up the yellow doll. "What do these eyes say to you?"

At first, they all looked the same, just painted eyes on wooden faces. But as I looked closer at the yellow doll compared to the pink one, I noticed the yellow had fewer eyelashes, making it look somehow sad or forgotten. I pointed this out to her.

"Yes!" she exclaimed, suddenly animated. "That's exactly it! You see it too."

She placed the yellow doll in front of herself and leaned toward me, her breath sour with alcohol. "Sophie, these are hand-painted and rare. Probably over one hundred years old. It makes them very special, but what's most remarkable is that whoever painted them is with us in this very room."

The kitchen seemed to shrink around us. My eyes darted to the corners, half-expecting to see some ancient Russian artist hiding in the shadows. The black doll in front of me suddenly seemed threatening with its blood-red lips. I pushed it away.

"No!" she snapped, shoving it back toward me. "There's a lesson here. You picked that doll, and now you must learn from it." She pushed it closer until it was inches from my face. "What is the doll saying to you?"

I began to cry, confused and frightened. If this was a game, I didn't understand the rules. But she kept demanding an answer, her voice rising.

"I'm a witch, and I'm evil," I finally shouted, desperate to end whatever was happening.

She sat back, satisfied. "Just as I thought."

That night marked a turning point. I began to understand that my mother's reality wasn't like other people's. In her world, dolls spoke, and colors had meaning beyond what eyes could see. And most importantly, I learned that sometimes the only way to survive was to play along, to give her what she wanted, even if it made no sense to me.

After that night, the collection continued to grow. For birthdays, Christmas, even random Tuesdays when she'd had a bad day or a good day or just needed to feel something, more dolls would appear. The curio cabinet became crowded, and she started keeping some in her bedroom. Sometimes late at night, I'd hear her moving them around, talking to them in low tones.

My father ignored it all. When she'd excitedly show him a new acquisition, he'd nod absently and return to his newspaper or phone call. Once, when I was about eight, I overheard him on the phone with someone, probably his sister, saying, "It's just dolls. Could be worse. At least they're not alive and need feeding."

But they were alive to my mother. And in a way, they became alive to me too, harbingers of her moods, witnesses to our strange family dynamic.

By the time I was nine, I could gauge her emotional state by which dolls she brought out. The black one with the red lips meant she was feeling dark, possibly dangerous. The yellow one often preceded crying jags about her lost ballet career. The pink one, ironically, usually meant anger was coming, a storm behind a pretty facade.

I developed my own system for navigating these waters. If she brought out the black doll, I'd be extra agreeable but find a reason to go to my room early. The yellow doll meant I should sit with her, be attentive, but not too cheerful, mirror her melancholy. The pink doll was the most dangerous; I learned to be wary, speak carefully, and keep physical distance.

She began to test me in other ways too. She'd arrange different dolls and ask what I thought they were feeling, what stories they were telling. I became adept at reading her cues, the tilt of her head, the tightness around her mouth, to determine what answer would please her.

"This one is jealous of that one," I might say, pointing at two dolls. "She thinks the other one is prettier."

"Yes, precisely," she'd respond, delighted that I was playing along, that I could see what she saw.

Looking back now, I realize I was being groomed, not in the way people usually mean that word, but groomed nonetheless, to accommodate her delusions, to validate her distorted view of reality. I was learning that truth was malleable, that what mattered wasn't what was real but what would keep the peace.

The Russian dolls were just objects, of course. Wood and paint and varnish. But they represented something more insidious: the idea that appearances hide truths, that inside every person are smaller, hidden versions of themselves, some darker than others. They taught me to look beyond surfaces, to anticipate the concealed motives of others. It was a valuable lesson, perhaps, but a terrible one for a child to learn so young.

Sometimes I wonder if I should thank her for that early education in human complexity. Other times, I think it robbed me of something essential, the ability to take things at face value, to trust.

When people show you who they are, Maya Angelou said to believe them the first time. But my mother showed me something different: that people are like those dolls, layers upon layers, and you never know what's hiding inside until you break them open.

Chapter 4: A memorable childhood experience I actually enjoyed?

What was a memorable childhood experience I enjoyed? This is the thing, I always had great hopes for myself, meaning I looked forward to doing things like skateboarding, swimming, and going on rides. But I hated them all. I was either piss poor at them or got scared. I don’t like being scared. It’s my Achilles heel and something I need to work on. But whenever I challenge myself and fail, the scary inside me gets more resistant and weightier, like some anvil wrapped around my waist.

Anyway, I tried to make the fear and anxiety go away. I thought I had found the key. But, of course, it was remarkably cliche and definitely in the cards given mother’s propensity. To be specific, I got drunk. However, at age ten, it may have been a little premature. But I’m getting ahead of myself.

I was in the fifth grade, and the class was getting clicky. Groups of boys and groups of girls formed and hung out. And then there were the ones who didn’t fit into either group—me and Ruby. We became great friends. Intimate, I’d say—telling each other secrets, talking about our families. She was hugely smart and tough. I admired her for both things. Her mom wasn’t with her father, and she had younger brothers. My parents were together, and I was an only child. She was black and American. I was white and French. She was badass. I was mauvais cul. We loved each other, held hands publicly, and discussed our future. She wanted to be an environmental scientist, and I wanted to be a gypsy. We had a lot of firsts.

Like the time, I was called gay by Pauly, a sixth grader. He liked Ruby. He called her “his puddin’ pie,” and I was “that gay girl.” Of course, it made little sense when you considered Ruby and I were best friends. But then again, boys at that age never grasped nuance.

That year, Ruby and I became blood sisters and made an oath to each other. She didn’t want to do the blood thing, so we sacrificed an animal. We cremated an already dead goldfish on her mother’s grill and spread its remains beneath a rosebush. We pledged to watch each other’s back forever.

Not long after that, there was the kiss.

It happened when we had a snow day from school, and Ruby came to my house. My father was at work, and my mother was gone. My snow day didn’t affect her routine. On Wednesdays, Racine always walked to the corner tavern for lunch. Everyone knew her there, and she was a big hit. My mother was beautiful and enjoyed flirting with men. I may have already mentioned that.

Unsupervised, Ruby and I were hanging around watching a TV cooking show. Innocent, right? Well, kinda, except after watching them make a rum cake, we looked through the cupboards and decided, rather than bake, why not dunk Oreo cookies into whiskey? So we each did a line of Oreos, about twelve, I’d say. And suddenly, the world became a different place. Hard edges became smooth. Colors became liquid. And inside, I felt like a big fluffy cloud. Ruby reacted differently. She got weepy and emotional and told me I was her best bestie. She then pulled me close and said she loved me. That’s when we kissed. Her lips were so soft. Everything about Ruby was full, ripe, dark, and deep.

Long story short, in one brief afternoon, I followed in my mother’s footsteps and lost my best friend.

Chapter 5: Do I know why I’m here?

Do I know why I’m here? Sure, my mother sent me. Said I was drunk and out of control. Ironic that she’s not sitting here beside me. What the heck has she been exposing me to all my life? Shouldn’t it be understood I’m the duckling who imprinted, not the duck who led the way? Who’s the crazy here? Besides, moving to France wasn’t my idea. Neither was the divorce. The point is drinking is makes me happy. You got AA meetings here for twelve-year-olds? It may be a better fit. No offense.

Oh, so this serves as an AA meeting? Great. My name is Sophie, and I’m an alcoholic. Your turn… Oh, you don’t have a problem?… Aren’t these meetings supposed to have more than one person? I rest my case. Rather than talk, why don’t I draw a picture with those crayons you have over there, and we’ll discuss it? This way, you can work on your computer while I draw. Give me a time frame, say 15 minutes….

Maybe they aren’t our own. Maybe they are bleed-throughs from another dimension or a past life remnant getting mixed up with the waking hours. On the other hand, they could be indigestion taking its toll during REM. Anyway, it would be good to get a second opinion. But I digress.

So here it is. You may note I’m not an incredible artist. Now, this would be me from my vantage point. They were not so curlicued and large in the dream. And that’s all I could do in 15 minutes. I kept erasing and changing the direction of the floorboards. Thoughts? Interpretations? Questions?

None. Oh. So you want me to interpret my dream? Sure. But I seem to be doing all the heavy lifting.

Okay. I’ll try to remain clinical, removed. I think four things are significant: the bedroom, the window, the bed, and the cat. Now, what is a bedroom? It’s a place to rest, to sleep, and, dare I say, to dream. Also, could be a room to escape into, to feel safe. Now the window is an opening. It’s the only opening. There’s no door. It’s a way out or in. The bed is the repository for a sleeping body, but no one is sleeping in the bed. The blue blanket reminds me of the sky. Finally, the cat. It’s the money shot, the most exciting aspect of the drawing. It’s hiding under a perfectly made bed. What kind of cat doesn’t want to lie in bed? What is it afraid of? What am I afraid of? Wow, this dream interpretation is powerful.

Your thoughts? None? Well, then maybe we could split the fee for these sessions. I could use the cash.

Chapter 6: In full disclosure…

Chapter 7: Welcome to the club where up is down, down is up

Welcome to the club where up is down, down is up, and it’s not Wonderland.

I once knew a guy, a father to a fellow student, who manufactured a world to his liking, which, to be honest, is not the worse thing. However, it became problematic when his world was on a collision course with mine…

Chapter 8: I was ten when my parents divorced

I was ten when my parents divorced. It was great. The warm-up to the divorce was the problem. For the three previous years, there was a lot of drama. They both took lovers. That’s what they called them, ‘lovers.’ We’d be sitting at the dinner table, and one would say. “Can you go to the teachers’ conference on Thursday? And the other one would answer, “No, I’m meeting my lover.” And the other would say, “Well, can’t you go a little later? The conference starts at six.” And an argument would ensue, not about being a lover or having a lover, but about lover time and how scheduling and rescheduling were problematic. Thinking back on it, I wonder if it was a paid service or a quid pro quo thing. However, since my mother didn’t work, I suspect a lover’s pool was aggregated from the marriage counselor’s clients. The counselor had questionable ethics.

Her name was Dr. Delilah. She was quite the card. Everything boiled down to sex: getting too much, too little, too dull, too rough, too fast, too slow, yada yada. I got to see her, aka Dr. D, after one of my mother’s ‘lovers’ wanted me to join them in bed. Yeah. I lived among crazies. Anywho, at the big powwow with my parents in Dr. D’s office, she explained that there was no such thing as pedophilia since we are all sexual beings from cradle to grave. Had I joined them? No, I ran from the room, went into the kitchen, grabbed a knife, and returned to stabbing and slashing with gusto, ending the affair. He never came back. My father wanted to contact the police. On the other hand, my mother argued with my dad and agreed with Dr. D., stating I was a resilient and resourceful child. Ergo, no harm, no foul. The only irreparable damage was a signed and shattered Capodimonte lamp.

Eventually, things got sticky when my father actually fell in love with one of his lovers, by the name of Shyanyana. I got to know her. Her birth name was Aggie. The poor thing was a mess. But I think that’s why my dad liked her. While he wasn’t particularly heroic, it was nice pretending to be.

Of course, my mother was a mess too, but differently. It’s like a spectrum thing. There’s the spectrum, then the edges of the spectrum on the far sides. Shyan and my mother were at opposite ends of normalcy in every imaginable way: one drank, one did meth; one screamed, one cried; one ate organic, and one drank Coke. I took this to mean finding women who were cheap dates. I know karma may bite me back for assuming the worst of my dad. But that’s life, open for interpretation.

Chapter 9: I arrived in France with a suitcase and my mother

I arrived in France with a suitcase and my mother. She enjoyed traveling light. The plan was to move in with her parents and slowly bring over valuables (Russian nesting dolls, Blahniks, Dali’s promotional jewelry) as we, or she, or my father, traveled by plane for visits, holidays, legal affairs, and art openings. Did I mention my mother had a freelance job? She went to galleries, took videos, and submitted them to art dealers who had buyers on both sides of the Atlantic. With the internet, she did well in the emerging artist field, charging a 20% finder’s fee.

Anyway, my mother had mixed feelings about living in the States. She loved the attention but never found friends, female friends. American women were whiners or slobs. Yeah, Racine looked at things with a broad stroke and wasn’t one for nuance. France suited her better—the cafe life, the flitting about, the kiss kiss. But her parents were problematic. I learned this after moving in with them. What’s the saying?… a crouton is just a piece of bread.

Mamie was my grandmother, and Papi was my grandfather. Everything about them was perfect and particular and exotic. My father’s family was a whole other story–parochial, like a fifties kitchen, dated, vaguely retro but uncool. I was a snob. I learned it from my mother. But since I rarely listened to her, I take some responsibility for this.

Before the move to France, I knew my maternal grandparents, like I knew desserts, special treats never to gorge on, and if you did, there were consequences. I saw them a few times a year. They doted on me in a formal, particular way that I enjoyed. I felt like an adult with them. They never pandered to me by speaking English, only French. My French was awful, but I understood every word they said. When I responded in English, they’d politely ask, s’il vous plait parlez français. Between attending a regular school and going with them to cafes and their friends’ homes for dinners, I was fairly fluent in six months.

Some days when they had a special event, they kept me from school to rencontrer des gens intéressants (meet exciting people). Well, I was enthralled. Yeah, I was a weird kid. I wanted to experience life, not be on my phone all the time. When they referred to me as being assez précoce (quite precocious), I thought I was the bomb, so much so that I started smoking. They were amused.

My mother? Oh, she didn’t care. She was off at the cafe meeting friends, usually of the male persuasion. Perhaps some backstory would be good at this point.

I’ve often wondered about ancestral energies, but I think my maternal side of the family were one-offs. Before then, the families of both my French grandparents lived on farms in the country. My grandparents met at church, married, and moved toute suite to Paris, where Papi went to college, became a radical, dropped out, then went into insurance. Mamie studied ballet for years into her thirties. She started late, at eighteen, and couldn’t get serious about doing it for a living. To this day, she walks around in gauzy, floaty things. I have to say, in her defense, the woman’s posture is remarkable. Anyway, before giving birth to my mother, Mamie and Papi drove around Paris in a Triumph and, from what I can tell from the photographs, lived a French movie life. You know, smoking, drinking, sitting in pretty surroundings with other couples (slender men with skinny ties; women with smokey eyes and poofy hair) who looked straight out of central casting. Which, by the way, many were. The movie industry in Paris was taking off, and they were always looking for extras.

Eventually, my mother was born, and their wings were clipped. Well, that’s not quite right. My grandmother’s wings were clipped. My grandfather just kept rollin’ along. So cliche, mais non? Anyway, their little darling Racine started properly at the Paris Opera Ballet school at age eight and became a ballerina. They had great hopes for her. Unfortunately, Racine had other ideas. While she continued to take ballet classes, she secretly wanted to become an actress. After years of grueling rejections, often recounted while drunk to the gills, my mother finally accepted her fate and settled into becoming an actress in her world. But, in her defense, haven’t we all?

Chapter 10: You want to return to the Paris years?

You want to return to the Paris years? No prob. Let’s see where did I leave off? Oh yes…

For the first year, living with my grandparents was exciting. Everything was new, even the air, its oxygen, and how it affected my body and mind. It felt like a deep cleanse, a realignment of sorts. You know when you’re on vacation, walking down a strange street, and suddenly your mind gets refreshed, rebooted? Everything’s brighter, cleaner, and you feel calm, mythic, and aligned as if you are on the edge of discovery. You’ve felt that, right? No? Hmm. You must only go to Cancun.

Anyway, I dove into my Parisian life remarkably well. Of course, my mother was happier. Her drunks were more festive, effusive, and dramatic. She danced, she sang, and she became desperately busy. She also lost weight and didn’t come home. What I thought was happiness was the inclusion of cocaine into her diet. Duh.

Not surprisingly, my mother and grandparents’ relationship had eroded by the second year. My only interaction with her was at the regular Sunday dinner at my grandparents’ home. Racine would bring dessert and her hanger-on du jour, sometimes male, sometimes female. The best was this snake-tatted guy with a small Sunbeam snake, a weird iridescent thing, in his pocket. He said he didn’t go anywhere without the snake, and he, the snake, was part of the package. With little response, and before drinks were served, Mamie went into the kitchen, pulled out the roasting pan, and dumped the entire chicken–aromatics, potatoes, et al.–into a pillowcase and sent them on their way. Yes, a pillowcase. There was never any plastic in the house, and a paper bag would only break. Anyway, it was a drag since she made the best chicken and knew her herbs.

I continued to attend school, but my absences increased as I spent more time with my grandfather. The school sent my homework to me by email on Thursdays. No questions were asked, no answers needed. Papi must have told them I had a standing appointment with someone on Fridays. He was quite the resourceful fellow who liked to push the boundaries. He would often say: Les normes sociétales étaient destinées aux crétins, pas aux libres penseurs. (Societal norms were meant for morons, not freethinkers.)

Anyway, we became simpatico, thick as thieves, when I was thirteen. I gained full access to his library. No book was off-limits. First print editions of Sartre, Camus; Bemelmans’s MADELINE, a ton of art books, from tapestries to erotica. Some were translations. Others, I painstakingly picked through using online translators. He would mention books I subsequently devoured, like Anaïs Nin’s diaries, that we’d discuss during our trips to various cafes he frequented. Yes, often on Fridays when there would be less traffic, and he had to take a break from Mamie.

The more I read, the better my vocabulary became. Before long, I was taking his advice on what clothes to wear. What made me look frumpy, sophisticated, too old, too childish, too sad, too ridicule. At the cafe, we’d look at Vogue France together. He’d point to an ad or model and ask, “ce qui est bien, ce qui est mal,” what is good, what is terrible? I took him seriously. He had great taste, and I was finally getting attention, undivided and private—secrets we could share. The worst part of this year was that, ever so slowly, I learned not to challenge anything he said and abdicated the little power I had. Of course, I only realized this in hindsight when we took the trip to Nice.

Yes, undoubtedly, he had an ulterior motive. Oh, the life of an ingenue. And the beat went on.

Chapter 11: The trip to Nice was a Christmas gift

The trip to Nice was a Christmas gift from both my grandparents; only Mamie had no intention of going. The holidays always wore her out, and her best present would be a quiet January to recuperate, do yoga, and have Pachelbel play 24-7. Papi hated Pachelbel. He referred to him as the Warhol of the 17th Century, whose compositions were insipidly simple and redundant. My grandparents took out their frustrations on each other, not by arguing but by expressing an extreme distaste for what the other one liked. For example, in retaliation to her husband’s Pachelbel rant, Mamie would parry, lunge and call jazz the trailer trash output of heroin addicts. Of course, I had the sense to stay out of it and listen to my playlist of Ani DiFranco and Natalie Merchant with my AirPods.

Anyway, Papi and I spent a lot of time planning the trip. He wanted to meet up with some of his old friends. One lived in Milan, and the other in Barcelona. They knew each other in the sixties when they were in their radical phase. Nice was a good midway point for all of them and especially beautiful in the winter. I was looking forward to it since I got along well with the Parisian crew, who were a fun group, always talking about politics, books, and women. Gave me a peephole into how men thought, or at least French men of a certain age.

When I was first brought around, they were a little standoffish. Maybe I was too. Soon, they started asking me questions, and we were having conversations. However, one guy, Raul, wasn’t won over so quickly.

At one point, when Raul came late and had to sit next to me, he asked me what I liked to do. I said I enjoyed reading, drinking coffee, and smoking. He then asked if I played chess. I told him I learned from my father. He went behind the bar, got a chess set, and challenged me to a game. I wasn’t sure if the gesture was friendly or sinister. He was indifferent toward me.

Before my first move, I asked him, “Do you want to lose, or do you want to win?”

He got a kick out of that. “But of course, I want to win.”

In twenty minutes, he had me in checkmate. His pompous ass won, but I could tell he realized his mistake by answering my question. The win wasn’t clean. Was it possible I let him win? Was it a one-off, a fluke?

“Let’s play one more,” he said, adding, “This time I want to lose.”

Fifteen minutes later, I had him in checkmate. I could tell by the bulge in the vein in his neck he was pissed. He had no intention of throwing the game by losing. His friends roared. He was a stubborn old guy.

He then wanted to play best of three.

I asked him again if he wanted to lose or win. He refused to answer and packed up the chess set.

I was relieved he folded. He played well, and I didn’t want to push my luck. We talked about the game the following week. Chess is a game of skill. But a little psych can turn the tables. Once that question was asked and answered, the game, whether won or lost, became tainted. It was a psych, a head game.

Anyway, Raul and I became great friends. He was more protective of me than my grandfather. He had my back, and that meant the world to me. Still does.

Chapter 12: Back to the trip?

Back to the trip? Yes.

Trips are exciting. There’s the planning, packing, the sense of adventure, and excitement if you’re open to it. Unfortunately, Papi wasn’t, and it surprised me.

On the morning of the trip, the talkative man with a quick smile and wit struggled from the first minute he woke up.

We left on a Thursday morning, thus upsetting his usual routine, no waking up to then dawdle in the bathroom, no feeding the birds, no going online to read the latest news or check the weather. He had to pack, which put him on edge.

When I walked into his room, he had laid out four days of clothing on the bed. It was the first time I realized how the man was not so much fastidious but conscious of his appearance and mask. I didn’t think that at the time. It came to me after four days of being with him.

I’ve often asked myself if it would have been better not to have gone. Four days, a blink of an eye, set me on an alternative path, specifically here. Who would have thunk? It was inevitable. Trip or no trip. I just didn’t know it at the time.

By early afternoon, we were cruising along the French countryside. He pulled out a small watercolor set, brush and suggested I do some paintings. “Look at the colors of the landscape and do wide swipes.” Sounded easy enough. Before then, I had shown no consistent interest in art. I opened my journal, found a clean page, and did as he suggested.

The countryside rolled by. As I tried to capture one scene, another took over—large vistas of rolling hills passed into tight clumps of trees that sped by. I kept two pictures going, one at the top of the page, the other at the bottom.

His first comment to me was to not mix the colors in the pans but to take dabs of paint and use the ridged underside of the tin as my palette. He also suggested I dry the watercolor nib between colors. I found an old tissue in my backpack and followed his directions. My first dismal page ended up puckered, soaked, and colorless.

“This will never do,” he said. “Try again.”

I felt a brief stab in my heart. He had never been so critical. I asked, “How can I get the color darker?” He opened the newspaper and didn’t answer. His reaction reminded me of my mother, his daughter, when I was left to my counsel. Point being I was used to it.

I used less water on my next attempt and swirled the brush deep into the pan. The paint became thicker, and the color denser. I then laid the watercolor pen onto the page, squeezing the cartridge to dilute the paint as I pulled the brush across the paper. The result was an improvement, but hardly anything pleasant to look at or interesting to continue. I ripped the page out of my journal, cleaned the pen, and slapped the top shut on the watercolors.

He looked over the newspaper. “You’re quitting?”

I said yes, but I wanted to challenge him, to tell him if he wanted me to do what he asked, he needed to show me first.

“Learning can be challenging,” he said. “It’s important not to give up unless you’ve committed to at least try, not dabble. Dabbling erodes your confidence.”

“Why are you asking me to paint? I’ve never known you to paint.”

“Of course, I’ve painted. For years. I’ll show you some of my work that’s put away.”

“Why don’t you show me now? I’d like to see how you do it.”

“Don’t be difficult,” he said and returned to the newspaper.

My brief excursion into painting was a revelation. Say no when you don’t have the slightest interest and lead your own friggin’ life.

We rode in silence the rest of the way.

Chapter 13: What happened when you arrived in Nice?

We pulled up to the hotel around 4:00 pm. After the silent car ride, I was ready to be anywhere that wasn't that suffocating vehicle. The Hotel Negresco stood like a wedding cake against the blue Mediterranean, all white façade and pink dome. It was the kind of place my mother would have photographed for her art dealers, exclaiming how it was "so Belle Époque, so quintessentially French Riviera."

The doorman, wearing a uniform with more gold braid than a dictator's formal wear, opened my door before I could reach for the handle. Another man materialized to take our luggage. Papi tipped them both generously, his earlier sourness evaporating in the coastal air. He'd transformed back into the charming gentleman everyone admired, everyone except me at that moment.

"Sophie, isn't this magnificent?" he asked, sweeping his arm toward the marble lobby with its massive chandelier. "This is how people should live."

I nodded, not trusting myself to speak. The hotel was beautiful, yes, but something felt off. Maybe it was the way Papi had suddenly brightened, like someone had flipped a switch inside him. Or maybe it was how the receptionist looked at me when Papi checked us in, her eyes flickering with something like concern before her professional mask slipped back into place.

"Monsieur Beaumont, welcome back. Your usual suite is ready." She handed him two key cards. "And for mademoiselle?"

"My granddaughter will be sharing the suite, of course. The second bedroom."

The receptionist's eyes met mine briefly again. "Of course. Enjoy your stay."

Our suite was on the fifth floor, with windows facing the sea. Two bedrooms connected by a sitting room with antique furniture that looked too delicate to use. My room had a canopied bed and French doors opening to a small balcony. In any other circumstance, I would have been thrilled.

"Freshen up, Sophie. My friends will be meeting us in the lobby at six. Wear something nice." He disappeared into his bedroom without waiting for my response.

I unpacked my clothes, laying them on the bed. Before the trip, Papi had insisted on taking me shopping, buying me dresses that were "appropriate for Nice." I fingered the silk of one, wondering why a fourteen-year-old needed cocktail attire for a family trip. The thought brought a chill despite the room's perfect temperature.

After a shower, I put on the least adult-looking of the dresses, a navy blue thing with a high neck and sleeves. I pushed my damp hair behind my ears and applied some lip gloss. Looking in the mirror, I realized with a start that I appeared older than fourteen. Not in a good way, in the way children forced to grow up too fast look older.

Papi was on the phone when I came out. "Yes, she's ready... No, no difficulties... Around twenty, I told them." He hung up when he saw me.

"Who was that?" I asked.

"No one important. My associates. Are you ready?"

I nodded, though ready for what, I wasn't entirely sure.

In the lobby, two men stood as we approached. One was tall, balding, with a thick salt-and-pepper beard that didn't quite hide the looseness of his jowls. He wore a linen suit and no tie. The other was shorter, completely bald, with skin like tanned leather and small, dark eyes that assessed everything.

"Emilio! Alejandro!" Papi embraced each of them with backslaps. "It's been too long."

"Indeed, Jacques, indeed," said the taller one in accented French. He must be Emilio, the one from Milan. His eyes moved to me, lingering a beat too long. "And this must be the granddaughter you've told us so much about."

Papi placed his hand on my shoulder. "Sophie, these are my old friends. Emilio Rossi from Milan and Alejandro Vega from Barcelona."

I shook their hands, noticing how both men held mine a moment longer than necessary. Alejandro's palm was dry and rough, his grip too tight.

"Enchantée," I said, pulling my hand back.

"The pleasure is all ours," Alejandro replied. His French was clipped and precise. "Jacques, she is even more lovely than you described."

Something twisted in my stomach. What exactly had Papi been saying about me?

"Shall we have a drink before dinner?" Papi suggested, already leading us toward the hotel bar.

The bar was dimly lit, with plush red chairs and small round tables. A pianist played quietly in the corner. We sat at a table near the windows overlooking the promenade. Night had fallen, and the lights along the coast glittered like stars had fallen into the sea.

"What would you like, Sophie?" Papi asked.

"Just water, please."

Emilio laughed. "Surely the young lady would like something more festive? A spritz perhaps? Or champagne?"

I shook my head. "Water is fine, thank you."

The waiter came, and the men ordered whiskey. Papi ordered me a San Pellegrino without asking if that's what I wanted. When the drinks arrived, the three men fell into conversation about old times, political movements, and mutual acquaintances. I sipped my water and watched.

"Remember Claudine?" Emilio asked, and they all laughed as if sharing a private joke.

"She was something," Alejandro agreed. "And that summer in Antibes..." His eyes slid to me and away.

"Perhaps not all stories are appropriate for young ears," Papi said, but he was smiling.

"Young ears often hear more than we think," Emilio replied, staring at me openly now. "Isn't that right, Sophie?"

"I suppose," I said, unsure how to respond. "Though I'm usually more interested in what I'm reading than in what others are saying."

"Ah, she has wit!" Alejandro exclaimed. "What do you like to read, ma petite?"

The "ma petite" grated on my nerves. "Lately, Anaïs Nin," I answered, watching their reactions. "Her diaries."

The men exchanged glances. Emilio's eyebrows rose. "Jacques, you allow her such... mature reading?"

Papi waved his hand dismissively. "Sophie has an old soul. She understands nuance beyond her years."

"Indeed," Alejandro murmured, his eyes now assessing me differently. "Most... impressive."

By the time we moved to the restaurant for dinner, my nerves were raw. The men had done nothing overtly inappropriate, yet every look, every comment seemed to carry hidden meaning. I felt like a specimen being examined.

The hotel restaurant had chandeliers dripping with crystal and tables set far enough apart for private conversations. A woman in a green dress at Alejandro's side greeted us as we approached their table. She had dark hair pulled back in a severe bun and sharp cheekbones that caught the light.

"Jacques, it's been too long," she said, kissing Papi on both cheeks. "And this must be Sophie." She turned to me with a smile that, unlike the men's, seemed genuinely warm.

"Sophie, this is Isabelle, Alejandro's wife," Papi introduced us.

"It's nice to meet you," I said, relieved to see another woman joining us.

"Come, sit next to me," Isabelle patted the chair beside her. I took it gratefully, putting distance between myself and Emilio, who had been trying to guide me to a seat between him and Papi.

"Have you been to Nice before?" Isabelle asked as we settled in.

"No, this is my first time."

"You must see the old town tomorrow. The market is wonderful. Perhaps I could take you while the men have their business discussions?"

Before I could answer, Papi cut in. "Perhaps another time. I have plans for Sophie tomorrow."

Isabelle's smile didn't waver, but something flickered in her eyes. "Of course. Another time."

The waiter arrived with menus, and the conversation shifted to food and wine. When it came time to order, Papi spoke for me without asking what I wanted, selecting a seafood dish I didn't particularly like.

"And to drink?" the waiter asked.

"The young lady will have, " Papi began.

"I'll have water, please," I interrupted.

Emilio laughed. "Come now, a little wine with dinner never hurt anyone. We're in France, after all."

"Indeed," Alejandro agreed. "A small glass of white with the fish would be appropriate."

I looked to Papi, expecting him to refuse on my behalf. Instead, he nodded. "A small glass of the Chablis, perhaps."

The waiter looked uncertain. "Monsieur, the young lady appears..."

"It's a special occasion," Papi said firmly.

When the wine came, I didn't touch it. Isabelle, noticing, quietly switched our glasses when the men were engaged in conversation.

"You don't have to drink it," she whispered. "I'll take care of it."

I gave her a grateful look. Throughout dinner, the men's conversation grew louder as they consumed more wine. Twice, Emilio reached across the table to touch my hand while making a point. Once, Alejandro commented on how "mature" I looked in my dress. Papi said nothing to discourage them.

"Sophie, tell Emilio about that book you were reading," Papi prompted during dessert. "The one with the interesting passages about the brothel."

My face flushed. I had indeed read Nin's descriptions of her time working in a brothel, but I'd never discussed those passages with Papi. How did he know I'd read them?

"I don't recall those parts specifically," I lied.

"Nonsense," Papi insisted. "You told me just last week how fascinated you were."

I hadn't told him any such thing. Was he trying to create a certain impression of me for his friends?

"Perhaps Sophie is tired," Isabelle interjected smoothly. "It's been a long day of travel."

"Not at all," Emilio said, refilling his glass. "The night is young. After dinner, we could go to that club you mentioned, Jacques."

"Club?" I asked, alarm bells ringing in my head.

"Just a place with good music," Papi said casually. "You'd enjoy it."

"Actually, I am tired," I said, forcing a yawn. "The drive was exhausting."

"Of course," Isabelle said before anyone could object. "A young girl needs her rest. There's always tomorrow."

As we finished dessert, I excused myself to use the restroom. In the hallway, I leaned against the wall, trying to calm my racing heart. Something was very wrong. The way these men looked at me, the things Papi was saying, it all felt like some horrible setup. I pulled out my phone, scrolling through my contacts. Raul had given me his number months ago, saying to call if I ever needed anything.

"I'll wait for Sophie," I heard Isabelle say as I approached the table. "Ladies' room." She met me in the hallway. "Are you alright?"

I nodded, not trusting my voice.

She glanced around, then leaned closer. "Listen to me. Lock your door tonight. Push a chair under the handle if you can."

"What?" I whispered, fear crystallizing in my chest.

"Just do it. And if you need help, my room is 312."

Before I could ask more, she straightened up and smiled brightly as Alejandro rounded the corner. "There you are! We were wondering if you'd gotten lost."

Back at the table, the men were discussing plans for the next day. Something about a boat, a private beach.

"You'll love it, Sophie," Emilio said, his words slightly slurred. "Very exclusive. Very... private."

When we finally returned to our suite, Papi was in high spirits. "Wasn't that a lovely evening? My friends were so impressed with you."

"Why would they be impressed with me?" I asked, standing by my bedroom door.

"You're an unusual girl, Sophie. Mature. Intelligent. They appreciate that."

"I'm going to bed," I said, retreating into my room.

"Sleep well," he called. "Big day tomorrow."

I closed the door and turned the lock, then dragged the desk chair over and wedged it under the handle. Sitting on the edge of the bed, I pulled out my phone and stared at Raul's number.

I needed help. I just wasn't sure who I could trust.

Chapter 14: Why did you call Raul that night?

I stared at Raul's number, my thumb hovering over the screen. The hotel room felt too quiet, too perfect, like a stage set where something terrible was about to happen. Outside my window, Nice glittered, oblivious. My hands were shaking.

Why did I call him? I keep asking myself that question, even now. Was it intuition? Some animal instinct about danger? Or was it just the culmination of a thousand tiny warnings I'd been ignoring for months?

Three months earlier, Raul had slipped me his number. We were at the café after another chess game, he'd won that one fair and square, and Papi had gone to the restroom. Raul leaned across the table, his voice low.

"Sophie, I want you to have my private number."

I blinked at him, confused. "Why would I need that? We only see each other here."

His eyes, normally cynical, had turned serious. "Because someday you might need someone who is not your grandfather."

"That's cryptic," I'd said, trying to sound casual.

"Just take it." He'd written the number on a napkin, then tucked it into my book. "Don't tell Jacques. Some things should stay between friends, yes?"

I'd shrugged and forgotten about it until I found the napkin weeks later while cleaning out my backpack. Something made me program it into my phone under the name "Chess Club." I never thought I'd actually use it.

But there I was in Nice, sitting on a canopied bed in the Hotel Negresco with a chair jammed against my door, my heart hammering like I'd run a marathon. I pressed call.

One ring. Two rings. Three.

"Allô?" Raul's gruff voice.

"It's Sophie." My voice sounded small, even to me.

A pause. "Are you alright?"

"I'm in Nice with Papi."

Another pause, longer this time. I heard ice cubes clink against glass. "Nice?"

"He brought me to meet his friends. Emilio and Alejandro."

Something changed in Raul's breathing. "Where are you specifically?"

"Hotel Negresco. We have a suite. Two bedrooms."

"Is your grandfather with you now?"

"No, he's in his room. I locked my door. And..." I glanced at the chair. "I blocked it."

"Good. That's very good, Sophie." His voice had turned calm, deliberate, like someone talking to a person on a ledge. "Did something happen?"

"Nothing specific, just... the way they look at me. Things Papi says about me to them. They tried to get me to drink wine at dinner. There's talk about a private beach tomorrow." My words tumbled out, sounding paranoid even to my own ears. "Alejandro's wife, Isabelle, told me to lock my door tonight."

"Listen to me carefully," Raul said. "You were right to call. I know these men. I know what they're capable of."

A chill ran through me. "What do you mean?"

"We'll discuss that later. Right now, you need to, "

A knock at my door cut him off. "Sophie?" Papi's voice, gentle. "Are you awake?"

I froze, phone pressed to my ear.

"Sophie, I heard voices. Who are you talking to?"

"Tell him you were watching a video," Raul whispered through the phone.

"Just watching something on my phone," I called, trying to sound sleepy.

"Open the door. I brought you some tea."

"I'm already in bed, Papi. Thank you, but I don't want any tea."

The familiar building now looked ominous, its ornate balconies and tall windows like the decadent facade of a haunted house. I wanted to run, but where would I go?

"I'll check on you tomorrow," Raul had promised, passing me a small card with another phone number. "If you need anything before then, call this number. Anytime, day or night."

The concierge barely glanced up from his newspaper as I shuffled through the lobby. The elevator seemed smaller than I remembered, its brass fittings tarnished in the corners where the cleaning cloth never quite reached. I pressed the button for the fourth floor, my finger trembling slightly.

What would I tell Mamie? The truth seemed impossible to articulate, a poison too dangerous to release into the air between us.

The apartment door was unlocked. I pushed it open to find Mamie in the living room, wrapped in one of her gauzy ensembles, a pale blue scarf floating around her thin shoulders. She was arranging flowers, snipping stems with small silver scissors, her movements precise and balletic even in this mundane task. Pachelbel played softly in the background, of course.

"Sophie?" She looked up, surprise flickering across her carefully maintained face. "What are you doing back so early?"

I stood frozen in the doorway, my small suitcase clutched in my hand like a shield.

"Where is your grandfather?" Her eyes darted past me to the empty hallway.

"He's still in Nice." My voice sounded strange, disconnected from my body.

She set down her scissors, wiping her hands on a small cloth. "I don't understand. You were meant to stay until Sunday."

"I came back early." The words felt inadequate, ridiculous.

Mamie crossed the room, her dancer's grace evident even in her seventies. She touched my forehead with the back of her hand. "Are you ill?"

The gentle contact made me flinch. Her hand dropped away, confusion clouding her eyes.

"No, I'm fine. I just... wanted to come home."

"Alone? On the train? Without telling anyone?" Her voice rose slightly. "Sophie, this is not acceptable behavior. Your grandfather must be worried sick."

I almost laughed at that. Worried sick about his ruined plans, perhaps.

"He knows I left," I said, which wasn't entirely a lie. "I'm tired from the journey. May I go to my room?"

She hesitated, clearly wanting to press for more information, but her ingrained sense of propriety won out. "Very well. But we will discuss this properly when you've rested."

My bedroom felt both familiar and foreign, like revisiting a place you've only seen in dreams. I dropped my suitcase and collapsed onto the bed, still in my clothes from Nice. The ceiling above me had a small crack that branched out like a river delta. I'd spent countless nights staring at that crack, imagining it was a map to some other place, somewhere far from here.

I must have fallen asleep because when I opened my eyes again, the room was dark except for the glow of streetlights filtering through my curtains. Voices drifted from the living room, Mamie on the phone, her tone hushed but urgent.

"...completely unexpected... No, she won't say... I don't know what to think, Jacques..."

So she was talking to him. My stomach clenched. What story had he concocted? What lies was he feeding her right now?

I slipped from my bed and crept to the door, pressing my ear against it.

"...of course I'm worried... No, she didn't mention Isabelle at all... Yes, tomorrow then."

The click of the phone being replaced in its cradle. Footsteps moving away. I waited until I heard Mamie's bedroom door close before venturing out.

The apartment was dark except for a small lamp in the living room. On the coffee table sat Mamie's cordless phone and a half-empty glass of something amber, unusual for her. I picked up the glass and sniffed. Cognac. So Mamie had needed fortification for her conversation with Papi.

I took a sip, wincing at the burn. It wasn't the first time I'd tasted alcohol, but it was the first time I'd reached for it knowingly seeking its numbing effects. I drained the glass, feeling the warmth spread through my chest, softening the edges of my anxiety.

In the kitchen, I found the bottle on the counter. I poured two fingers into Mamie's glass and carried it back to my room, drinking it slowly as I sat cross-legged on my bed.

The alcohol created a buffer between me and reality, a thin membrane through which the horrors of Nice seemed slightly less immediate. I thought about Isabelle on the train platform, creating a diversion so I could escape. About Raul meeting me at the station. About the mysterious envelope I'd carried back to Paris.

What was happening now? Were Papi's "friends" still looking for me? Was Isabelle safe? The questions swirled in my head, blurring as the cognac did its work.

I must have dozed off again because I was awakened by the sound of the apartment door opening. Morning light streamed through my window. Voices in the foyer, Mamie and someone else. A man's voice, low and controlled.

My heart stopped. Papi was back.

I scrambled from bed, looking frantically around my room. Where could I hide? What could I do? The burner phone Isabelle had given me was in my bag, but Raul hadn't expected Papi to return so quickly. Neither had I.

"Sophie?" Mamie called. "Are you awake? Your grandfather is home."

I stood paralyzed, unable to respond. My mouth was dry, my head throbbing from the cognac and lack of proper sleep.

A soft knock at my door. "Sophie?" Mamie again.

"I'm not dressed," I called, my voice cracking.

"We're waiting in the living room when you're ready," she replied. "Don't dawdle. We need to talk."

I moved like a sleepwalker, washing my face, brushing my teeth, pulling on clean clothes. Each ordinary action felt surreal, as if I were watching someone else perform them. In the mirror, my face looked pale, with dark circles under my eyes. I looked haunted. I was.

When I finally emerged, they were sitting side by side on the sofa like a parental united front. Papi looked tired but composed, his clothes slightly rumpled from travel. He smiled at me, actually smiled, as if nothing had happened.

"There she is," he said warmly. "Our little runaway."

Mamie patted the armchair across from them. "Sit, Sophie. Your grandfather has explained everything."

I perched on the edge of the chair, poised for flight. "Has he?"

"I understand now why you left so abruptly," Mamie said, her voice gentle. "And while I wish you had handled it differently, I can see why you were upset."

I stared at her, then at Papi. What possible explanation could he have given?

"It was a misunderstanding," Papi said, his voice the same one he used when discussing art or literature with me. Reasonable. Thoughtful. Utterly false. "My friends can be overwhelming, especially Emilio. His comments about your reading material were inappropriate."

"Comments about my reading?" I repeated, disbelieving.

"Yes, at dinner. You became uncomfortable when Emilio suggested you were too young for certain books. Then when we discussed the beach trip and mentioned it was a private area frequently visited by nude sunbathers..." He sighed dramatically. "I should have been more clear that you wouldn't be expected to participate in that aspect. It was insensitive of me."

I couldn't believe what I was hearing. He had rewritten the entire Nice experience into a simple misunderstanding about nudity at a beach.

"You looked so distressed, I should have realized immediately," he continued. "Instead, I dismissed your concerns, and for that, I apologize."

Mamie nodded encouragingly. "We all make mistakes, Sophie. The important thing is that your grandfather recognizes his error in judgment."

"That's why you ran away? Because you thought we were taking you to a nude beach?" Papi asked, his expression concerned yet somehow amused, as if this were a charming misunderstanding between generations.

The audacity of his fabrication left me speechless. How could I possibly counter this narrative? How could I tell my grandmother that her husband of fifty years had planned to offer me to his pedophile friends? That it wasn't about books or beaches but about something unspeakably worse?

"Sophie?" Mamie prompted. "Your grandfather is apologizing. What do you have to say?"

I looked between them, feeling utterly alone. Mamie's expectant face. Papi's carefully crafted mask of contrition. The weight of family history and obligation pressing down on me.

"I..." My voice faltered. The truth hovered on my tongue, desperate to escape. But then I saw it in Papi's eyes, a flash of warning, of threat. If I spoke now, who would believe me? What evidence did I have beyond my own suspicions and the word of people Mamie had never met?

"I overreacted," I said finally, the words tasting like ash. "I'm sorry for causing worry."

Papi's shoulders relaxed slightly. "All is forgiven," he said magnanimously. "Though perhaps in the future, you'll speak to me directly about your concerns rather than running off and involving strangers?"

"Strangers?" Mamie asked, frowning.

"A woman helped me get back to Paris," I said cautiously. "Isabelle."

"Ah yes, Alejandro's wife," Papi said smoothly. "She misunderstood the situation as well. Though why she felt it necessary to put you on a train without consulting me..." He shook his head regretfully. "It created quite a panic when we discovered you were gone."

I bet it did, I thought bitterly.

"Well, the important thing is that you're both home safe," Mamie said, clearly eager to restore normalcy. "I've prepared a nice lunch, and perhaps later we can all visit that exhibition at the Pompidou you were interested in, Sophie."

The idea of spending an afternoon pretending everything was normal made my stomach turn. "Actually, I'm not feeling well. I think I need to rest more."

Mamie's face fell, but she nodded. "Of course. You've had a trying experience."

As I stood to return to my room, Papi spoke again. "Oh, Sophie? That number you called from Nice, Raul's number? I'd appreciate if you'd delete it from your phone. He's not the sort of influence a young girl should have."

Our eyes met across the living room. In his, I saw calculation and warning. In mine, I hope he saw defiance.

"I'll consider it," I said, and fled to my room.

Once inside, I leaned against the closed door, heart racing. He was back. He was here, in the same apartment, sleeping just down the hall. And he'd managed to rewrite reality so completely that Mamie accepted his version without question.

I pulled out the burner phone Isabelle had given me and texted Raul: "He's back. Told Mamie it was all a misunderstanding about a nude beach. She believes him."

The reply came almost immediately: "Stay calm. Don't confront. We're working on it."

Working on what? How long would I have to live under the same roof as a monster? How many more family dinners and polite conversations could I endure?

My gaze fell on my school bag in the corner. Inside the smallest pocket, wrapped in a sock, was a flask I'd stolen from an older student months ago. I'd filled it with vodka taken in small amounts from my grandparents' liquor cabinet, intending it for some future rebellion.

The future was now.

I unscrewed the cap and took a long swallow, then another. The sharp burn faded to warmth, then to blessed numbness. The fear receded, not gone but dulled, manageable.

This was how I would survive, one sip at a time, building a wall between myself and the truth too terrible to face sober.

Chapter 18: Welcome to the club where up is down, down is up

I'd spent my life watching adults lie. My mother, my father, then my grandfather, master manipulators all. So when the academic counselor, Madame Perrault, showed me my midterm grade report with an expression of profound concern, I simply stared at the cascade of failing marks with detached curiosity. Like watching someone else's life implode.

"Sophie, your performance this term is... concerning." She tapped the paper with a manicured fingernail. Red, like the marking pen that had slashed through my essays. "You've gone from near the top of your class to..." She trailed off, gesturing helplessly at the evidence.

I should have cared. Two months ago, I would have. Before Nice, before discovering what lay beneath my grandfather's cultured facade. Now school seemed like an elaborate joke. Verb conjugations and quadratic equations, meaningless symbols in a world where monsters wore expensive cologne and called themselves family.

"I've been distracted," I offered, knowing she expected some response.

"Distracted." She pronounced the word like it was a diagnosis. "For seven weeks? Sophie, your literature essay consisted of a single paragraph in which you wrote, and I quote, 'Like Meursault, I find it hard to care when the sun is in my eyes.' That was it. Nothing about Camus' existentialism, nothing about the novel's themes."

I shrugged. "It's a valid interpretation."

Madame Perrault removed her glasses, pinching the bridge of her nose. "Your mathematics teacher says you haven't submitted homework in three weeks. In chemistry, you mixed substances that were clearly labeled as dangerous when combined."

"I was curious about the reaction."

"You nearly caused an evacuation!" She leaned forward. "Sophie, I've known you since you arrived in Paris. This isn't you. What's happening?"

What's happening is I'm living with a predator who my grandmother worships. What's happening is I've learned the world is a lie wrapped in silk. What's happening is I can only sleep when I'm drunk enough not to dream.

"Nothing's happening," I said. "I just don't see the point anymore."

She studied me with professional concern. "Is there trouble at home?"

I nearly laughed. Trouble at home? Home was trouble, distilled to its essence.

"Everything's fine." The lie slid off my tongue with practiced ease. "May I go now? I'll try harder on the next assignments."

She sighed, scribbling a note. "I'm arranging regular sessions with the school psychologist. And I'm calling your grandmother."

I nodded, already planning how I'd intercept that call. The phone in my grandparents' apartment rang in three places: the living room, kitchen, and my grandfather's study. Mamie never answered my grandfather's line, and I could monitor the other two.

Outside Madame Perrault's office, the corridors bustled with students changing classes. I blended into the flow, another uniform in the stream, invisible by choice. I'd perfected the art of being present but unseen, my gaze fixed slightly above everyone's heads, my expression neutral enough to discourage approach.

"Impressive performance in there." A voice beside me, low and amused. "I counted six different lies, but I might have missed some."

I turned to find a girl I vaguely recognized. Tall, rail-thin, with short black hair and eyes lined with smudged kohl. She wore the required uniform but had somehow altered it to look like a costume she was wearing ironically.

"I wasn't performing," I said. "And I wasn't lying."

"Please." She rolled her eyes. "I know a professional when I see one. I'm Camille, by the way."

"Sophie."

"Oh, I know who you are. Everyone does. The American-French girl who set fire to the chemistry lab."

"I didn't set fire to, "

"Details." She waved dismissively. "The point is, you've made an impression. The good girls whisper about you in the bathrooms. They think you're dangerous."

"I'm not dangerous," I said automatically, though somewhere inside, I wondered if that was true anymore. What would I be capable of, if pushed?

"No, you're not," Camille agreed, studying me. "You're wounded. Much more interesting."

Something in her assessment pierced through my carefully maintained apathy. This girl saw me, not the facade I presented, but something beneath.

"What do you want?" I asked.

"Company." She shrugged. "I'm having a smoke behind the gymnasium during lunch. Join me if you want."

Before I could respond, she melted into the crowd, leaving me staring after her. I should have been suspicious of her interest. Instead, I felt a flicker of something I'd nearly forgotten, curiosity.

At lunch, I found myself wandering toward the gymnasium. Around the back, partially hidden by recycling bins, Camille sat cross-legged on the ground, smoke curling from a cigarette between her fingers. She wasn't alone. Two older boys lounged against the wall beside her.

"She came," Camille announced as I approached. "Told you she would."

The taller boy assessed me with lazy interest. "This is the one you mentioned? Doesn't look like much."

"Neither did I, when you first met me," Camille retorted. "Sophie, meet Luc and Thibault. They graduated last year but can't seem to leave us poor children alone."

Luc, the tall one, offered a mock salute. Thibault merely nodded, his gaze uncomfortably penetrating. They were perhaps eighteen, with the studied carelessness that takes actual effort to maintain.

"Want one?" Luc extended a pack of cigarettes.

I hesitated. I'd smoked before, of course, it was practically mandatory for French teenagers. But always in private, away from watchful school eyes.

"We won't get caught," Camille said, reading my hesitation. "The gym teacher is having an affair with the music teacher. They're too busy during lunch to patrol."

I accepted a cigarette, and Luc lit it with a silver lighter engraved with initials not his own. The smoke burned pleasantly, a different sensation from the alcoholic numbness I'd grown accustomed to.

"So," Thibault spoke for the first time, his voice unexpectedly gentle. "Camille says you're interesting."

"Camille is mistaken."

"I'm never mistaken about people," Camille countered. "You're carrying something heavy. It's in your eyes."

I looked away, uncomfortable with her perception. "We all have problems."

"True." Luc took a long drag. "But some problems require more creative solutions than others."

From his pocket, he produced a small metal flask, offering it to me. I recognized the gesture for what it was, a test, an invitation into whatever circle they'd formed.

I should have walked away. But walking away meant returning to classes that felt meaningless, to a home where danger slept down the hall, to a reality I couldn't bear to face sober.

I accepted the flask, taking a swallow without asking what it contained. Vodka, cheap but effective, burning a familiar path to temporary peace.

Thibault watched me with new interest. "Not even a question about what you're drinking. Interesting indeed."

"I'm not picky," I said, passing the flask to Camille.

"A girl after my own heart." Luc grinned. "We're getting together tonight. Little gathering at my place. Parents are in Geneva. You should come."

"Both of you," Thibault added, glancing at Camille.

I hesitated, thinking of the emptiness waiting at home. Of the careful dance around Papi at dinner, pretending everything was normal. Of the flask hidden in my sock drawer, diminishing faster than was sustainable.

"I have a curfew," I said.

"Everyone has a curfew." Luc shrugged. "That's what windows are for."

After they left, Camille and I remained, sharing a final cigarette.

"You don't have to go tonight," she said, surprisingly gentle. "They can be intense."

"Why did you introduce me to them?"

She studied the cigarette between her fingers. "Because you have the look."

"What look?"

"The one that says you're searching for an exit door." She met my eyes. "I recognized it. It's the same one I see in the mirror."

That night, I told Mamie I was going to bed early to study. She seemed relieved by this return to academic diligence, not questioning why studying required a closed door and solitude. Papi was out at a "meeting," a blessing that made my escape easier.

At 10:30 pm, I climbed out my bedroom window onto the narrow ledge that ran alongside the ornate façade of our building. It wasn't my first time navigating this particular escape route. The stone gargoyle three feet to the left made a perfect handhold to reach the service ladder, which descended to the inner courtyard.

Camille waited on the street corner, smoking nervously. "You came," she said, sounding surprised.

"You didn't think I would?"

"Wasn't sure. You seem like someone who weighs decisions carefully."

Once, perhaps. Now I moved on impulse, chasing anything that might drown out the screaming in my head.

Luc's apartment was in the 16th arrondissement, a sprawling space with marble floors and art I recognized as valuable. About fifteen people had gathered, most older than us, some clearly university students. Music pulsed from expensive speakers, loud enough to energize but not so loud as to disturb the neighbors.

"Drink?" A girl with impossibly long legs handed me something blue in a crystal glass. I didn't ask what it was, just swallowed it in two gulps. She raised an eyebrow, impressed or concerned, I couldn't tell.

The night blurred pleasantly. More drinks appeared whenever my hand was empty. Someone passed around pills, small white tablets that Camille took without hesitation. I declined those, alcohol was familiar, controllable in its effects. Pills were an unknown variable.

"Smart," Thibault said, suddenly beside me. He hadn't taken the pills either. "Better to know what you're losing yourself in."

"I'm not losing myself," I protested, though the room had begun to tilt at interesting angles.

"No?" He smiled, not unkindly. "Then what are you doing here, Sophie Jamison?"

I didn't remember telling him my last name.

"Same as everyone else. Having fun."

"That's what they're doing." He nodded toward the others, dancing, laughing, kissing in corners. "You're self-medicating. Different objective entirely."

Something about his assessment, so similar to Camille's earlier perception, irritated me. "You don't know me."

"I know the type. My sister was the same way. Something happens, something that breaks the world open, and suddenly nothing makes sense anymore. Up is down, down is up. Welcome to the club."

"What happened to your sister?" I asked, curious despite myself.

His expression shuttered. "She found a permanent solution to a temporary problem."

Before I could process that, a commotion erupted near the door. Two uniformed police officers stood in the entrance, surveying the suddenly silent room.

"Noise complaint," one announced. "Everyone under eighteen, line up. We're calling parents."

Panic surged through my alcohol haze. If they called my grandparents, if Papi came to collect me...

I locked eyes with Camille across the room. Without a word, she nodded toward a hallway. I followed, slipping past a bathroom where someone was being noisily sick, into a bedroom, and through French doors onto a small balcony.

"Fire escape," she whispered, pointing to a metal structure. "Quick."

We descended in silence, dropping the last few feet into an alley behind the building. Sirens wailed in the distance, more police coming, or an ambulance for whoever had overindulged inside.

"This way." Camille grabbed my hand, pulling me through a maze of back streets until we emerged near the Seine, both breathless and slightly giddy from our escape.

"That was close," I gasped, adrenaline temporarily burning through some of the alcohol.

"Too close." She grinned, but it faded quickly. "My mother would kill me if she knew. Or worse, be disappointed. Again."

"Mine wouldn't notice," I said before I could stop myself. "She's too busy with her own disasters."

Camille studied me in the amber glow of a streetlight. "We're quite a pair, aren't we? The invisible girl and the disappointment."

"I'm not invisible."

"No?" She cocked her head. "Then why hasn't anyone at school noticed you're drowning?"

The question hit like a slap. I turned away, staring at the black water of the Seine sliding past. "Maybe I don't want to be noticed."

"Bullshit. Everyone wants to be seen. Just... by the right people. People who understand."

"Like you?" I asked, my tone more sarcastic than I'd intended.

"Maybe." She sounded uncertain for the first time. "I recognize the signs because I've been there. Still am, most days."

"Where's 'there'?"

"The place where everything's backward. Where adults who should protect you don't. Where you're screaming inside but smiling outside. Where the only peace comes from a bottle or a pill or a blade."

Her words resonated so perfectly with my experience that I felt momentarily dizzy, as if the world had shifted beneath my feet.

"How do you get out?" I whispered.

Camille laughed, a hollow sound. "If I knew that, I wouldn't be climbing out windows to get drunk with strangers."

We made our way back to our respective homes in comfortable silence, parting with a promise to meet at the same spot behind the gymnasium tomorrow.

As I climbed back through my bedroom window at 2:40 am, I found a note slipped under my door. Madame Perrault's handwriting:

"Meeting tomorrow, 10:00 am. School counselor's office. Attendance mandatory. Your grandmother has been notified."

So Mamie had answered Madame Perrault's call after all. Tomorrow would bring questions, concern, the pressure to explain my academic freefall. They would want answers I couldn't give, solutions I didn't believe in.

I crumpled the note and reached for the flask in my sock drawer. Empty. Of course.

Pressing my forehead against the cool window glass, I watched Paris sleeping below, its lights twinkling like false promises. Tomorrow they would try to fix me, not understanding that what was broken wasn't me, but the world itself. A world where predators lived in plain sight, protected by wealth and reputation, while their victims learned to swallow their screams with vodka.

Welcome to the club where up is down, down is up, I thought, echoing Thibault's words. And there are no exits, only temporary escapes.

Chapter 19: What happened when your mother returned?

I was hiding in my room, nursing a hangover and dreading my impending meeting with the school counselor, when I heard the front door slam open. For a terrifying moment, I thought Papi had returned early from his "business meeting" with his deplorable friends. But then I heard a voice I hadn't heard in weeks.

"Maman! I'm home! Where's my Sophie-baby?"

My mother. Racine. Home from wherever she'd disappeared to this time.

I pressed my palms against my eyes, willing the throbbing in my temples to subside. Perfect timing, as always. The universe had a sick sense of humor, sending my mother back now, when I was barely holding myself together.

The click of heels across the parquet floor, growing louder. My grandmother's voice, hushed but sharp. "Racine! We weren't expecting you. You look, "

"Fabulous? I know!" My mother's laugh, too loud, too bright. The sound of kissing, exaggerated smacks on both cheeks.

I stood, catching my reflection in the mirror. Pale face, dark circles, hair unwashed. I looked exactly how I felt: hollow. I pinched my cheeks to bring some color to them and ran my fingers through my hair, a pathetic attempt at preparation.

The knock came seconds later. Three rapid taps, her signature.

"Sophie? Chérie? It's Maman!"

I opened the door, and there she was, resplendent in her chaos. My mother wore a vintage Chanel suit in electric blue, her blonde hair freshly highlighted, sunglasses pushed up on her head despite the fact it was 8:30 am on a cloudy day. Her smile was too wide, her pupils pinpricks in a sea of blue.

"There's my girl!" She engulfed me in a hug that smelled of Shalimar, cigarettes, and something chemical beneath. "God, I've missed you! Let me look at you."

She held me at arm's length, her expression shifting from delight to concern so quickly it was almost comical. "You look terrible. Are you sick? Not eating? Maman, why does she look like this?"

Mamie appeared behind her, looking significantly less pleased about this reunion. "Perhaps because her mother vanished for three weeks without a word?"

My mother waved this away. "I told Papa where I was. Sort of. Anyway, I'm back now!" She focused on me again. "We have so much to catch up on. Let's do breakfast, just us girls. There's a new place in Saint-Germain that serves the most divine avocado toast."

"I have school," I said flatly. "And a meeting with the counselor."

"Counselor? What for? Are you in trouble?" She seemed almost excited by the prospect.

"Sophie's grades have fallen dramatically," Mamie said, her tone clipped. "And there was an incident in chemistry class."

"Chemistry! That was always my worst subject too." My mother laughed. "Don't worry, I'll straighten everything out. I'll come with you to this meeting."

The thought of my cocaine-energized mother charming the school counselor filled me with dread. "That's not necessary."

"Of course it is! What kind of mother would I be if I didn't support my daughter in her time of need?"

The kind you've always been, I thought but didn't say.

An hour later, I found myself sandwiched between my mother and grandmother in Madame Perrault's office. My mother had changed outfits, now wearing a more subdued gray dress that screamed "responsible parent." She sat with perfect posture, nodding earnestly as Madame Perrault detailed my academic collapse.

"I had no idea," she said, reaching for my hand across the armrests. I let her take it, though her touch felt like an intrusion. "Sophie has always been such a brilliant student."

"Indeed," Madame Perrault agreed. "Which is why this sudden change is so concerning. We've noticed other behaviors as well, skipping classes, appearing tired or disengaged, possible alcohol use."

My mother gasped dramatically. "Alcohol? Sophie? Impossible."

The irony of her outrage made my stomach turn. Just the night before, she'd probably been doing lines of cocaine off some stranger's coffee table.

"We're concerned that something may have happened," Madame Perrault continued. "A traumatic event perhaps, or changes at home?"

All three women looked at me. I stared at the carpet, tracing the pattern with my eyes. Squares within squares within squares. Boxes to put people in, to keep secrets in.

"Nothing happened," I said automatically.

My mother squeezed my hand. "Sophie, honey, if something's bothering you, you can tell us. Is it because I've been away? I know I should have called more, but I was working on this huge gallery installation in Amsterdam, and the time just got away from me."

Amsterdam? That was new. Last I'd heard, she was supposedly in Lyon.

"It's not that," I said.

"Is it about the trip to Nice?" Mamie asked softly. "Your grandfather told me what happened. The misunderstanding."

I felt my mother stiffen beside me. Her hand tightened around mine, nails digging slightly into my palm.

"Nice?" she asked, her voice suddenly tight. "What trip to Nice?"

"Papa took Sophie for a few days," Mamie explained. "To meet some old friends. But there was a... misunderstanding about appropriate activities, and Sophie came home early."

My mother's face had gone very still, a trick I'd seen her perform at parties when someone said something that cut too close to the bone. She'd freeze, perfect as a porcelain figurine, while her mind raced behind that placid mask.

"Which friends?" she asked, her voice deceptively casual.

"Emilio and Alejandro," I said, watching her carefully. "And Alejandro's wife, Isabelle."

Something flickered in my mother's eyes. Recognition. Fear. Then nothing, her mask back in place.

"I see," she said. Then, turning to Madame Perrault with a brilliant smile: "Clearly, we need to give Sophie some stability. I'll be staying in Paris indefinitely now. We'll get her back on track, won't we, chérie?"

Madame Perrault seemed satisfied with this declaration of maternal dedication. She outlined a plan for tutoring sessions and regular check-ins, which my mother enthusiastically endorsed. I sat silently, watching this performance unfold, wondering how many times in my life adults had made promises around me that they never intended to keep.

Afterward, my mother insisted on taking me to lunch, just the two of us. She dismissed Mamie with airy assurances that we'd be home for dinner. We walked to a café several blocks from school, her arm linked through mine like we were girlfriends rather than mother and daughter.

"So," she said once we were seated, menus open before us. "Tell me about Nice."

I studied her over the top of my menu. "I thought you knew those men. Papi's friends."

She signaled the waiter. "Two sparkling waters, please. And are your mussels fresh today?" After securing the waiter's assurance about the mussels' freshness, she turned back to me. "I know of them, yes. I haven't seen them in years."

"But you know what they're like."

She busied herself arranging her silverware into perfect alignment. "I don't know what you mean."

"Yes, you do." I lowered my voice. "Papi was going to... he wanted me to..."

"Stop." She held up her hand. "Just stop, Sophie. Whatever you think happened, or was going to happen, you're mistaken. My father is a respected businessman."

"Who takes his fourteen-year-old granddaughter to meet his pedophile friends."

The sparkling water arrived. My mother took a long sip, her throat working. When she set the glass down, her hand was trembling slightly.

"You have a vivid imagination," she said finally. "Always have. Reading those books, filling your head with outlandish ideas."

"Raul warned me about them. Isabelle helped me escape."

My mother's eyes narrowed. "Raul? That communist friend of Papa's? What does he have to do with this?"

"He protected me when you wouldn't." The accusation hung between us, sharp as a blade.

She leaned forward, her voice dropping to a fierce whisper. "Listen to me very carefully, Sophie. You will not repeat these accusations to anyone. Do you understand? It would destroy our family."

"Our family?" I laughed, a hollow sound. "What family? The mother who disappears for weeks? The grandfather who, "

"Enough!" She slammed her palm on the table, making the silverware jump. Several nearby diners turned to look. My mother immediately plastered on a smile, waving apologetically. When she spoke again, her voice was controlled, pleasant. "You're confused, Sophie. Maybe it's the drinking, yes, I can smell it on you, don't look so shocked. Or maybe it's just teenage rebellion. But these stories you're telling yourself are dangerous."

"They're not stories."

"They are." She reached across the table, taking my hand in both of hers. "Listen, I know it's been hard. The divorce, the move to France, me being away so much. But making up these... these fantasies about your grandfather won't solve anything."

I stared at her, seeing for the first time what she really was: not just neglectful, not just selfish, but complicit. She knew. She'd always known.

"He did the same to you, didn't he?" I whispered. "That's why you left France. Why you drink. Why you disappear."

Her face drained of color. For a moment, I thought she might actually tell me the truth. But then the waiter appeared with our mussels, and the moment shattered. By the time he left, her mask was firmly back in place.

"You need help, Sophie," she said, spearing a mussel. "Professional help. I'll speak to a psychiatrist friend of mine."

I didn't touch my food. "I'm not crazy."

"I didn't say you were. But these delusions, "

"They're not delusions." I stood up, my chair scraping loudly. "And I don't need another adult telling me I'm wrong about what I know happened."

I walked out, ignoring her calls to come back. Outside, the Paris streets were filled with tourists and locals going about their day, unaware of the rot beneath the city's elegant surface. I started walking, no destination in mind, just away from her, from the lies, from the creeping realization that no one would ever believe me.

Three blocks later, I noticed she was following me. Not obviously, my mother was too practiced a performer for that, but I caught glimpses of her electric blue suit reflected in shop windows, hovering at crosswalks, sliding between pedestrians.

Fine. She wanted to follow me? I'd give her something to see.

I cut through a side street, then another, until I reached a part of the Marais I'd discovered during my solitary wanderings. A decrepit building stood half-hidden behind construction barriers, abandoned mid-renovation when the developer went bankrupt. Local teenagers had found ways past the nominal security, using it for drinking and graffiti and God knows what else.

I slipped through a gap in the barriers, then through a broken window at the back. Inside, it was cool and dim, dust motes dancing in the beams of light that penetrated the boarded windows. The walls were covered in layers of graffiti, some artistic, some obscene. Empty bottles and cigarette butts littered the floor.

I positioned myself near a window where I could see the street, waiting. Sure enough, five minutes later, my mother appeared, looking confused and increasingly worried as she scanned the street. She walked past the construction barriers twice before noticing the gap I'd used.

I watched as she hesitated, her pristine Chanel suit distinctly out of place in this gritty setting. Then, surprisingly, she squeezed through, grimacing as the rough edges caught at her clothes.

"Sophie?" she called, her voice echoing in the empty space. "Sophie, I know you're in here. This isn't funny."

I stayed silent, curious how far she would go to find me. She picked her way carefully through the debris, swearing quietly when her heel caught in a crack.

"This is ridiculous," she muttered. "Sophie! Come out right now!"

When I still didn't respond, she pulled out her phone. I expected her to call me, but instead, she dialed another number.

"Jacques? It's me. We have a problem." She paused, listening. "No, she knows. About Nice. About your friends." Another pause. "Of course I denied it! What was I supposed to do?" She began pacing, her free hand gesticulating wildly. "Well, maybe if you hadn't taken her there in the first place! What were you thinking? She's fourteen, for God's sake!"

I leaned forward, straining to hear my grandfather's response, but I could only make out the tinny sound of his voice, not his words.

"I don't care about your arrangements," my mother continued. "This is my daughter we're talking about. My daughter!" She stopped pacing, her face contorting. "Don't you dare throw that in my face. I was a child. She is a child."

She fell silent, listening. Then, her voice dropping to a whisper I could barely hear: "No. No, I won't let you do to her what you did to me. I'm taking her away from here. Far away."

My breath caught. For the first time in my life, my mother was standing up for me. Protecting me. I felt a rush of something unfamiliar, hope, maybe, or the ghost of it.

But then my grandfather must have said something convincing, because my mother's posture changed. Her shoulders slumped, her free arm wrapped around her waist.

"I know. I know." Her voice was small now. "You're right. Where would we go? What would we live on?" A longer pause. "Yes. I understand. I'll handle it. She'll understand eventually."

She ended the call and stood motionless in the center of the room, staring at nothing. Then, with mechanical movements, she opened her purse and removed a small vial. Tilting her head back, she tapped white powder onto the back of her hand and inhaled it quickly.

The transformation was immediate. She straightened up, adjusted her suit, ran a hand through her hair. The vulnerable woman of moments ago vanished, replaced by the polished, confident Racine I knew.

"Sophie," she called again, her voice steadier now. "This game has gone on long enough. Come out, and we'll forget this whole thing happened."

I stepped from my hiding place, slow applause echoing in the empty space. "Brava, Mother. Truly an Oscar-worthy performance."

She spun toward me, surprise quickly masked by relief. "There you are. You had me worried sick."

"No, I didn't. You were never worried about me. Only about what I might say, who I might tell."

She sighed dramatically. "We're not doing this again. I told you, these stories, "

"I heard you on the phone," I interrupted. "With Papi. I heard you admit it. 'I won't let you do to her what you did to me.' Those were your exact words."

The color drained from her face for the second time that day. "You misheard."

"No, I didn't. And I saw what you just did. The cocaine. Does that help you forget what he did? Or just help you function despite remembering?"

She took a step toward me, something dangerous flashing in her eyes. "You have no idea what you're talking about."

"I think I do. He hurt you, and instead of protecting me from him, you left me with him. You knew what he was capable of, and you still left me there."

"It wasn't like that." Her voice cracked, the cocaine confidence already wearing thin. "I didn't think he would... not with you. His own granddaughter."

"His own daughter didn't stop him, did it?"

She slapped me. The sound echoed through the empty building, sharp as a gunshot. We both froze, equally shocked by the violence of it. My cheek burned, but the pain was almost welcome, physical, tangible, honest in a way nothing else in my life was.

"I'm sorry," she whispered, reaching toward me. "Sophie, I didn't mean, "

I stepped back, out of her reach. "Don't touch me. Don't ever touch me again."

Something broke in her expression. For a moment, I saw not my mother but a damaged girl who had never escaped her own trauma, who had spent her life running from demons she couldn't outrun.

But I couldn't afford to feel sympathy. Not anymore. Not when my own survival was at stake.

"I'm going home now," I said, my voice steady. "And tomorrow, I'm going to school. And I'll keep going, day after day, pretending everything is normal. Because that's what we do in this family, isn't it? We pretend. We lie. We look the other way."

"Sophie, "

"But I won't forget. And I won't forgive. And someday, when I'm old enough, I'm going to walk away from all of you. And I'll never look back."

I left her standing there, surrounded by broken glass and graffiti, as shattered and defaced as the building itself. As I walked home through the gathering dusk, a strange calm settled over me. The truth hadn't set me free, if anything, it had confirmed my cage was even stronger than I'd thought.

But knowing the truth gave me something else: clarity. No one was coming to save me. Not my mother, not my grandmother, certainly not my father an ocean away. If I was going to survive this, I would have to save myself.

And to do that, I would need control. Control over my environment, my emotions, my future. No more reckless drinking, no more academic sabotage, no more blind rebellion. Those were luxuries I couldn't afford.

I would bide my time. I would play their game, outwardly compliant while keeping my real self hidden, protected. I would find allies where I could, build resources, create escape routes.

And one day, when they least expected it, I would break free.

Chapter 20: Tell me about the abandoned building

I found the abandoned building three weeks after returning from Nice, during one of my aimless walks through Paris. These walks had become my salvation, moments of freedom when I could exist without the weight of Papi's presence or Mamie's willful blindness pressing down on me. I'd leave school and wander for hours, sometimes missing dinner entirely. No one seemed particularly concerned by my absences. Papi was often "working late," and Mamie had mastered the art of not asking questions.

It was a Tuesday afternoon in late October when I first noticed the gap in the construction barrier. The building stood in a forgotten corner of the Marais, six stories of weathered limestone with ornate cornices and elaborate window frames that spoke of former grandeur. A faded sign announced plans for luxury apartments, but the thick layer of dust on the developer's logo suggested those plans had died long ago.

Curiosity pulled me toward the gap, just wide enough for a slender person to slip through if they turned sideways and didn't mind sacrificing a bit of clothing to the jagged edges. I hesitated only briefly before squeezing through, drawn by the promise of somewhere no one would think to look for me.

Inside, it was like entering another world. The ground floor had been gutted for renovation, leaving a vast space with soaring ceilings and exposed beams. Dust motes danced in shafts of amber light that streamed through boards covering windows. The air smelled of plaster, mildew, and something older, history perhaps, or secrets. Graffiti covered portions of the walls, some of it surprisingly beautiful: an enormous blue phoenix rising from concrete ashes, a woman's face with eyes that seemed to follow you, intricate patterns in vivid colors that must have taken hours to create.

I explored cautiously that first day, testing floorboards before committing my weight, avoiding the areas where water damage had left ominous stains spreading across the ceiling. A grand staircase with a wrought-iron railing led to the upper floors. Most of the second floor had collapsed in on itself, but at the far end, I discovered a single room that remained largely intact.

It must have been a library or study once. Built-in shelves lined one wall, and a large bay window overlooked a small courtyard now overrun with weeds. The window was cracked but not boarded over, allowing natural light to flood the space. In one corner, someone had left an old armchair with the stuffing spilling out of split seams.

I sat in that chair for nearly an hour, listening to the building settle and creak around me. For the first time since Nice, I felt something close to peace.

"I need to make this mine," I whispered to the empty room, and the walls seemed to whisper back their approval.

The next day, I returned with supplies: a flashlight, a blanket stolen from our linen closet, a notebook, and a bottle of vodka I'd pilfered from Papi's liquor cabinet. Day by day, I added to my collection. A pillow. A battery-powered lantern. Books I couldn't risk keeping at home. A box of colored markers. Candles, though I was careful with those at first.

Within two weeks, I'd created a sanctuary. I'd swept the floors with a small brush I'd found abandoned in a corner. I'd wiped down the shelves and arranged my small collection of treasures on them: interesting rocks and bottles I'd found on my walks, a ceramic figurine of a cat that reminded me of the one in my dream, a postcard of Amsterdam that I'd bought to remind myself of my mother's lies.

Camille was the only person I told about the building. She came with me once, bringing cigarettes and a portable speaker that filled the room with music.

"It's perfect," she said, spinning slowly in the center of the room. "Like something from a film. The ruined princess in her tower."

I scoffed at that. "I'm hardly a princess."

"No?" She looked at me with that penetrating gaze of hers. "You carry yourself like royalty in exile. All that quiet dignity hiding the rage underneath."

I threw a bit of plaster at her, and she laughed, the sound echoing through the empty building.

"You should write it down," she said later, as we shared a cigarette by the window. "All the things you can't say out loud. It helps."

That night, I tried writing in my notebook, but the words felt trapped there, imprisoned between lines. Instead, I took a marker and walked to the blank wall opposite the bookshelves. In large black letters, I wrote: "THEY DON'T BELIEVE ME."

Something broke open inside me then. I grabbed more markers and began covering the wall with words. Some were just single terms: "FEAR." "RAGE." "TRAPPED." Others were fragments of thoughts: "Why didn't she protect me?" "I remember everything." "Nobody sees the truth."

By dawn, I'd filled half the wall, my hand cramping, my fingers stained with ink. I stepped back and looked at my work, a chaotic collage of my inner life made external. It wasn't art, not like the phoenix downstairs, but it was honest. Raw. Real.

After that, the writing wall became my true therapy. I'd come to the building almost daily, adding new thoughts, crossing out old ones, connecting ideas with arrows and stars. Sometimes I'd write while completely sober, meticulously detailing memories I was afraid to forget. Other times, I'd drink until my handwriting became a slanting scrawl, pouring out feelings I couldn't acknowledge when clearheaded.

The wall began to take on a life of its own. I divided it into sections: one for memories of Nice, one for things my mother had said and done, one for observations about Papi and the way he manipulated everyone around him. In the center, I kept a growing list of plans for my future, cities I might escape to, jobs I might get, identities I might claim once I was free.

No one in my family noticed the changes in me. I'd become skilled at maintaining my facade, going to school, doing just enough homework to keep my grades acceptable, showing up for dinner with the appropriate amount of teenage sullenness but not enough to provoke concern. I'd even started seeing the school psychologist as required, feeding her carefully curated half-truths that satisfied her professional curiosity without revealing anything real.

Meanwhile, my true self existed only in that abandoned room, expressed only on that wall of words.

It was late November when it happened. The night was unusually cold, and I'd brought extra candles to warm the room. I'd also brought a full bottle of whiskey, stolen during one of Papi's dinner parties when the adults were too drunk to notice one more bottle disappearing from the already cluttered bar cart.

That day had been particularly bad. I'd overheard Papi on the phone with Emilio, making plans to meet in Berlin after the holidays. The casual way they spoke, as if Nice had never happened, as if they were just old friends catching up, had filled me with a rage so intense I could barely breathe through it.

In my sanctuary, I uncapped the whiskey and drank directly from the bottle, not bothering with the plastic cup I usually used. The liquid burned a familiar path down my throat. One swallow. Two. Three. The edges of the room softened. The rage didn't disappear, but it transformed, becoming something I could wield rather than something wielding me.

I grabbed my markers and approached the wall. In the flickering candlelight, my previous writings seemed to move and pulse like living things. I found an empty space and began to write, larger than before, pressing so hard the marker squeaked against the plaster.

"THEY WILL NEVER WIN. I WILL SURVIVE THEM. I WILL OUTLAST THEM. I WILL TELL THE TRUTH SOMEDAY AND EVERYONE WILL KNOW."

I drank more, the whiskey sloshing as I gestured with the bottle. My movements became less coordinated, my thoughts less coherent. I lit more candles, placing them on the windowsill, on the bookshelves, on the floor around me, creating a circle of light in which I could continue my furious writing.

I don't remember exactly what happened next. I remember dancing at some point, spinning with the whiskey bottle, watching the candlelight blur into streaks of gold. I remember crying, my tears falling onto the floorboards. I remember taking off my sweater because I was too hot, tossing it carelessly aside.

What I remember most clearly is the moment I smelled the smoke. Sharp, acrid, unmistakable. I turned to see my discarded sweater, now partially engulfed in flames where it had landed too close to a candle. Fire was already spreading to a pile of old newspapers I'd been using to clean the windows.

Fear sobered me instantly. I lunged for the sweater, trying to beat out the flames with my hands. This only succeeded in spreading the fire to the blanket I kept on the armchair. Within seconds, flames were climbing the wall, my wall of words, my private thoughts exposing themselves in the most public way possible.

I ran to the bay window and threw it fully open, hoping the cold night air would somehow help. Instead, the draft created by the open window caused the flames to surge higher, hungrily consuming my carefully constructed refuge.

Smoke filled the room, black and choking. I dropped to my hands and knees, crawling toward the door as I'd been taught in school fire drills. Behind me, I could hear the crackle and pop of burning wood, the crash of shelves collapsing. The heat pressed against my back like a physical force.

In the hallway, I staggered to my feet and ran for the stairs, descending them two at a time, barely registering the danger of the rotting steps. The ground floor was already filling with smoke too, the ancient timber of the building proving perfect kindling for the fire I'd started.

I burst through the broken window I'd first entered through all those weeks ago, tumbling onto the cold pavement outside. The shock of the fall knocked the breath from my lungs. I lay there gasping, watching in horror as orange flames became visible behind the upper windows of the building.

Somewhere in the distance, sirens began to wail. Someone must have seen the smoke, called the fire department. Soon they would be here. Soon there would be questions, authorities, consequences.

I scrambled to my feet and ran. The bottle of whiskey was still clutched in my hand, somehow unbroken despite everything. I hurled it into a dumpster as I passed, not wanting to be caught with it. My clothes smelled of smoke, my hands were blackened with soot, and my hair was singed at the ends. There was no way to hide what had happened, not completely.

Six blocks away, I finally stopped running, leaning against a wall to catch my breath. Reality crashed over me in waves. I had set fire to an abandoned building. I had destroyed the only safe place I had in this world. I had endangered not just myself but potentially others if the fire spread to neighboring structures.

I was in trouble. Real trouble, not the kind that could be smoothed over with lies and pretense. This wasn't something my family could make disappear with money or connections.

And yet, as I stood there listening to the distant sirens and watching the glow of the fire color the night sky, I felt a strange sense of release. My wall of words was burning, yes, but so were my secrets, my pain, my carefully hidden truths. The fire was destroying evidence that could have exposed me, thoughts too dangerous to exist outside my head.

Perhaps that was fitting. Perhaps my secrets were always meant to be transformed, not preserved, turned to ash and smoke that would rise above Paris and disperse into nothingness.

I straightened up and began walking toward my grandparents' apartment, rehearsing explanations in my mind. A school project gone wrong. A friend's party that got out of hand. A wrong place, wrong time coincidence.

I knew the truth would emerge eventually. The police would investigate. The school would be notified. My family would discover what I'd been doing. But for now, for these few moments as I walked through the dark streets of Paris, I was still the author of my own story, the keeper of my own secrets.

Tomorrow would bring consequences. Tonight, I was still free.

Chapter 21. Then you ended up in court?

Then you ended up in court? Yes, but not immediately. First came the knock on the door at 6:45 am, three days after the fire. I'd almost convinced myself I was in the clear. Almost.

I was the one who answered it, already dressed for school, hair carefully arranged to hide the singed ends. Two police officers stood in the hallway, a man and a woman, both with the studied neutrality that comes from years of delivering bad news.

"Sophie Jamison?" the woman asked. She had short brown hair and eyes that missed nothing.

My heart sank. "Yes."

"We'd like you to come with us to answer some questions."

Mamie appeared behind me, tying her silk robe with trembling fingers. "What is this about?"

"Are you the girl's guardian?" the male officer asked.

"Her grandmother. Her mother is asleep. What has happened?"

The female officer's gaze never left my face. "There was a fire in an abandoned building in the Marais three nights ago. We have reason to believe your granddaughter may have information about it."

Not "may have caused it." Just "may have information." They were being careful, giving me room to incriminate myself.

"That's ridiculous," Mamie scoffed. "Sophie was home that night."

I turned to look at her, surprised by the instinctive lie. Had she known I was gone? Had she been covering for me all along?

"Actually," I said, "I wasn't."

The hours that followed blur together in my memory. The police station with its harsh fluorescent lights and smell of industrial cleaner. The small interview room with a table bolted to the floor. The female officer, Detective Lambert, offering me water in a paper cup.

"We have security footage," she said, not unkindly. "From a shop across the street. You entering the building at 4:15 pm. You leaving at 11:37 pm. The fire was reported at 11:42 pm."

I stared at the table's surface, counting the rings left by countless cups. "I didn't mean to burn it down."

"Why don't you tell us what happened?"

So I did. A carefully edited version, a teenager seeking a private place, bringing candles for light, an accident with a discarded sweater. I left out the writing wall, the stolen whiskey, the months of using the building as my sanctuary. I made it sound like a one-time impulse, a stupid mistake.

"Were you alone?" Detective Lambert asked.

"Yes."

"Why that building? Why that day?"

I shrugged, aiming for typical teenage nonchalance. "It was empty. I just wanted somewhere quiet to think."

They exchanged glances, clearly not buying my simplified story. But arson investigators had already determined the fire was accidental, not deliberately set. The main issue was trespassing and the damage caused, nearly a million euros' worth to the building and smoke damage to adjacent properties.

By noon, my mother had arrived, a whirlwind of designer clothes and righteous maternal indignation. "I demand to know why my daughter was questioned without a parent present!" she declared to anyone who would listen.

"We attempted to contact her legal guardians," a desk sergeant explained wearily. "Your mother was present initially but chose to return home to contact you and your father."

"And no one thought to wait? She's fourteen!"

I sat on a bench, watching her performance with detached fascination. Racine paced and gestured, demanding to speak to supervisors, insisting on copies of all statements, throwing around terms like "procedural violations" and "rights of minors" she'd probably learned from American police shows.

Eventually, a tired-looking man in a rumpled suit appeared. He introduced himself as a representative from the prosecutor's office and explained I was being charged as a juvenile with criminal trespassing and negligent endangerment.

"What does that mean?" My mother demanded. "She's a child. It was an accident."

"It means," he said, "that she'll have a hearing before a juvenile judge next week. Given her age and the circumstances, she'll likely receive probation and mandatory counseling, possibly community service. But that's for the judge to decide."

They released me to my mother's custody with instructions to appear at the Palais de Justice the following Tuesday at 10:00 am. As we walked to her waiting taxi, she kept one arm wrapped protectively around my shoulders, for the benefit of anyone watching. Once inside the cab, she dropped her arm and turned to me, eyes glittering.

"Of all the stupid, irresponsible things to do," she hissed. "Do you have any idea how this looks? What people will say?"

"I'm sorry the family reputation is at risk," I said flatly.

"Don't be smart with me. Not now." She rubbed her temples. "Your grandfather is furious. Do you know the owner of that building is an acquaintance of his? He had to make calls to prevent this from becoming worse."

Of course he did. Papi and his connections, always manipulating events from behind the scenes.

"How much worse could it be?" I asked.

"They could have kept you in juvenile detention until the hearing. They still might, if you don't convince the judge you're suitably remorseful."

The taxi pulled up to our apartment building. My mother paid the driver while I stared at the ornate facade, wondering if it had ever truly been home. Would Papi be waiting upstairs? What new manipulation would he attempt now that I was in actual legal trouble?

But the apartment was empty. A note from Mamie on the kitchen counter explained she'd gone to her yoga class and Papi was at a meeting. Normal Tuesday activities, as if their granddaughter hadn't just been detained by police.

"Take a shower," my mother instructed. "You smell like that police station. Then we need to talk about your court appearance."

I did as I was told, standing under water hot enough to turn my skin red, wondering if I could wash away the last three days, the last three months, my entire life perhaps. When I emerged, wrapped in a towel, my mother was sitting on my bed, a notebook open on her lap.

"You need a narrative," she announced. "Something compelling but not pathetic. Something that generates sympathy without suggesting deeper issues."

"You mean I need a lie."

She ignored this. "I'm thinking school pressure. You've been struggling to adjust since moving from America. The cultural differences, language barriers, academic expectations. You sought solitude to clear your head, made a terrible mistake, and now you're devastated by the consequences."

"That's not what happened."

"It doesn't matter what happened. It matters what the judge believes happened." She tapped her pen against the notebook. "We'll need to address your appearance too. Something modest but not severe. The blue dress with the Peter Pan collar, I think. Hair pulled back. Minimal makeup."

I sat beside her, leaving careful distance between us. "You seem to have experience with this."

"I know how systems work. How people make judgments." She glanced at me. "And I know you're in real trouble, Sophie. This isn't a game."

For once, we agreed on something.

The juvenile courtroom was smaller than I'd imagined, with wood-paneled walls and fluorescent lighting that gave everyone a sickly pallor. My mother, grandfather, and grandmother sat in a row behind me. The prosecutor, a severe woman in her fifties, read the charges in a monotone that made setting fire to a historic building sound like a parking violation.

When it was my turn to speak, I rose on shaky legs. I'd refused to rehearse my mother's fabricated narrative, preferring to face whatever came with some shred of authenticity. The judge, a woman with kind eyes behind stern glasses, asked if I understood the charges.

"Yes, Your Honor."

"And do you wish to make a statement?"

I took a deep breath. Behind me, I could feel my mother's anxious energy, my grandfather's calculating gaze. "I did go into the building. I did accidentally start the fire with a candle. I'm sorry for the damage I caused and for putting others at risk."

"The report says you'd been in that building before. Multiple times."

I hesitated. This wasn't in the police report I'd seen. Had neighbors noticed me coming and going? Had I been less careful than I thought?

"Yes, Your Honor."

"Why?"

Such a simple question. So impossible to answer truthfully. I couldn't tell her about Nice, about Papi's predatory friends, about my mother's complicity. I couldn't explain that I needed somewhere to exist beyond my family's toxic influence.

"I needed space to think," I said finally. "Things at home have been... complicated since we moved from America. The building was quiet. I could be alone there."

The judge studied me, and I had the uncomfortable feeling she was seeing more than I wanted her to. "Your mother has written a letter asking the court to consider the cultural adjustment you've been experiencing. She suggests this may have contributed to your behavior."

Behind me, I could practically feel my mother preening at having her narrative acknowledged.

"Your school reports a significant drop in academic performance over the past few months, as well as behavioral concerns." The judge shuffled some papers. "Is there something else going on, Sophie? Something you'd like to tell the court?"

For a wild moment, I considered telling everything. Laying bare the rot at the heart of my family for this woman who seemed genuinely concerned. Would she believe me? Would it matter if she did?

But then I felt it, the weight of Papi's stare boring into my back. The subtle warning in its intensity.

"No, Your Honor. Nothing else."

She held my gaze a moment longer, then nodded, seeming to accept my answer while not quite believing it. "Given your age and the accidental nature of the fire, I'm placing you on probation for twelve months. You'll perform one hundred hours of community service and attend weekly counseling sessions. Your progress will be reviewed in three months."

She paused, her expression softening slightly. "Sophie, this court is giving you a chance to demonstrate better judgment. Please don't waste it."

As we left the courtroom, my mother squeezed my shoulders in a show of maternal support for anyone watching. "That went well," she whispered. "The judge clearly liked you."

I said nothing, focused on putting one foot in front of the other.

"The counseling is a nuisance," she continued, "but we can manage it. I know someone who can write up reports without you actually having to attend all those sessions."

"I'm going to the sessions," I said.

She looked surprised. "Why would you want to do that?"

Because I need someone to talk to, I thought. Because maybe, just maybe, a court-appointed counselor would have to listen if I told the truth. Because I can't keep carrying this alone.

But all I said was, "It's part of my probation."

Later, alone in my room, I pulled out a fresh notebook. On the first page, I wrote: "Court-Ordered Counseling: Session 1." Below that: "Things I Will Tell," followed by a short list of harmless admissions. On the opposite page: "Things I Won't Tell (Yet)," followed by a much longer list, beginning with "Nice."

The building might be gone, my wall of words turned to ash, but I could still document, still plan, still find a way forward through this maze of adult deceptions. Maybe the counseling sessions would provide an opportunity, a potential ally, a crack in the facade I could gradually widen.

Or maybe they would be just another dead end, another adult who wouldn't listen, wouldn't see.

Either way, I would be ready.

Chapter 22. Are our sessions confidential?

The waiting room of Dr. Eleanor Fontaine smelled like lavender and secrets. The walls were painted a calm sage green, adorned with generic watercolor landscapes that were probably supposed to be soothing. I sat on a leather chair that was just uncomfortable enough to keep me alert, counting the minutes on a wall clock that ticked too loudly.

I'd postponed this first court-ordered therapy session twice already. The first time, I claimed illness, and the second time, I simply didn't show up. Dr. Fontaine's secretary had called later that day, her voice pleasantly firm as she explained that missed appointments would be reported to my probation officer. So here I was, five days after my court appearance, trapped in lavender-scented purgatory.

A door opened, and a woman in her forties appeared. She had dark hair cut in a practical bob and wore a simple gray dress with a colorful scarf. No lab coat or clipboard, nothing that screamed "I'm going to analyze your every word."

"Sophie? I'm Dr. Fontaine. Please come in."

Her office continued the understated theme: bookshelves lined with psychology texts and literary fiction, two comfortable chairs facing each other, a small desk in the corner. No couch, thankfully. I'd prepared a whole routine about refusing to lie down.

"Water?" she offered, gesturing to a carafe on a side table.

I shook my head and remained standing, surveying the room. "Are our sessions confidential?"

If my bluntness surprised her, she didn't show it. "That's an excellent first question." She sat down, leaving me awkwardly standing. "Please, make yourself comfortable."

I remained on my feet. "You didn't answer me."

"You're right." She nodded. "Our sessions have qualified confidentiality. That means what you tell me stays between us unless you indicate you're planning to harm yourself or someone else, or you disclose ongoing abuse that legally requires reporting."

"And what about reporting to the court? Or my probation officer?"

"I'm required to confirm your attendance and provide general assessments of your progress. But the specific content of our conversations remains private."

"Written down where?"

Her eyebrow raised slightly. "In my confidential notes."

"Which the court could subpoena."

"That's technically possible, yes." She studied me with calm interest. "You seem concerned about what might be recorded. May I ask why?"

I finally sat down, perching on the edge of the chair. "Just establishing the parameters."

"Fair enough." She smiled slightly. "Any other questions before we begin?"

I pulled my phone from my pocket. "I'd like to record our sessions. For my own records."

This finally broke through her professional composure. She blinked, then recovered. "I'm afraid that's not something I can allow. It would compromise the therapeutic environment and potentially the confidentiality of our work."

"But you get to take notes." I held up my phone. "This would just level the playing field."

"This isn't a competition, Sophie." Her voice remained even. "My notes are professional documentation protected by privacy laws. Your recording would have no such protection."

"So if I can't record, and you won't show me your notes, how do I know what you're writing about me?"

"You can ask me at any time."

"And you'd tell me the truth?"

Something flickered in her eyes, recognition, perhaps. "Yes, I would."

I slipped my phone back into my pocket but didn't turn it off. The recording app was still running. A test, would she notice? Would she care enough to check?

"Why don't we start with how you're feeling about being here today?" she suggested.

"Thrilled," I said flatly. "Who wouldn't want to spend their Tuesday afternoons psychoanalyzing an accidental fire with a stranger?"

"Is that what you think we're here to discuss? The fire?"

I leaned back, crossing my arms. "Isn't it? That's why the court sent me to you."

"The court mandated counseling because they believe you could benefit from it. The fire may be part of what we discuss, but it's not the only focus."

"What else would we talk about? The weather? French politics? Your terrible artwork?" I nodded toward the bland watercolors on her wall.

She smiled, surprisingly genuine. "Not my choice, I'm afraid. The office came furnished." She tilted her head slightly. "We could talk about whatever's important to you, Sophie."

"Nothing's important to me."

"I find that hard to believe. You strike me as someone who cares deeply about many things."

I snorted. "You've known me for five minutes."

"True. So tell me something that would help me know you better."

I studied her, trying to decipher her game. Every adult in my life wanted something from me, compliance, silence, performance. What did Dr. Fontaine want?

"I set fire to a building," I offered. "That's the headline, isn't it? Troubled teen plays with matches, film at eleven."

"Is that how you see what happened?"

"It's how everyone else sees it."

"I'm asking how you see it."

I shrugged. "It was an accident. I've already admitted that in court."

"Accidents happen in context. You were in that building for a reason."

I wondered if she'd read the police report, if she knew about my sanctuary, my wall of words. How much had been documented before the fire consumed everything?

"It was quiet there," I said carefully. "I could think."

"What were you thinking about?"

I almost laughed. Where would I even begin? My predatory grandfather? My drug-addicted mother? The tangled web of lies that constituted my daily existence?

Instead, I deflected. "Don't you want to know about my childhood? Isn't that the standard procedure? Dig into my formative traumas, connect the dots to my delinquent behavior?"

"Is that what you'd prefer to discuss?"

"I'd prefer not to be here at all."

She nodded. "That's understandable. Few people choose therapy initially. But since we're both here for the next forty-five minutes, we might as well make use of the time."

I glanced at my watch. "Forty-two minutes now."

"Noted." She smiled again. "Why don't you tell me something about yourself that isn't in any report? Something you think I should know."

I considered fabricating something outlandish, claiming I was the reincarnation of Sylvia Plath or that aliens communicated with me through the radio. But honesty, even partial honesty, might be more disarming.

"I don't trust adults," I said. "Any of them."

"Any reason in particular?"

"Experience."

"Fair enough." She made a quick note. I tried to glimpse what she was writing, but her handwriting was illegible from my angle. "Have adults given you specific reasons not to trust them?"

"They lie," I said simply. "All of them. Some lie to protect themselves. Some lie because they think they're protecting you. Some lie because it's easier than the truth. But they all lie."

"All adults? No exceptions?"

I thought about it. Raul had been straightforward, but even he had kept secrets from me until it was almost too late. Isabelle had helped me, but I hardly knew her. "Maybe a few exceptions. But not many."

"Do you think I'm lying to you?"

"I don't know you well enough yet. But probably, eventually."

She didn't seem offended. "What about you, Sophie? Do you lie?"

"Of course. I learned from the best."

"When was the last time you told a significant lie?"

I smiled thinly. "How would you know if my answer to that question was itself a lie?"

"I wouldn't, necessarily. That's part of the trust we'd need to build."

"There's that word again. Trust." I leaned forward. "Let me ask you something, Dr. Fontaine. If I told you something truly disturbing, not about harming myself or others, just something deeply wrong happening in my life, what would you do?"

She held my gaze steadily. "That would depend on the specific nature of what you shared. If it involved ongoing abuse of a minor, including yourself, I'm legally required to report it. If it involved a past event or a complex family situation that doesn't meet reporting criteria, I would work with you to address it therapeutically."

"Very textbook answer."

"It's the truth, Sophie. I won't pretend there are simple answers to complex ethical questions."

I sat back, considering her. Unlike the other adults in my life, she didn't seem flustered by my directness or annoyed by my skepticism. She also hadn't tried to charm me or pretend we were instant friends. It was... refreshing, if still suspect.

"My phone is recording this session," I said abruptly, watching for her reaction.

She didn't flinch. "I thought it might be. Would you like to discuss why you felt that was necessary?"

Not the response I'd expected. "Aren't you going to demand I delete it?"

"I would prefer you didn't record our sessions, as I mentioned earlier. But I'm more interested in understanding why you felt you needed to."

"Evidence," I said. "In case you twist my words later."

"Has that happened to you before? Adults twisting your words?"

"More times than I can count."

She nodded thoughtfully. "That explains your concern about my notes as well."

"Are you going to report me to the court for recording you?"

"No, Sophie. But I am going to ask that moving forward, we agree not to record sessions. I believe we can establish enough trust that you won't feel it's necessary."

"Pretty optimistic of you."

"Perhaps." She glanced at the clock. "We have about twenty minutes left. Would you be willing to delete today's recording, as a first step?"

I pulled out my phone, stopped the recording, and pressed delete. "There. Happy?"

"Thank you." She paused. "You know, you could have just pretended to delete it."

"I could have," I agreed. "But I didn't."

Something shifted subtly between us in that moment, not trust exactly, but a recognition of each other's intelligence. She knew I was testing her, and I knew she was allowing it, for now.

"For our remaining time," she said, "I'd like to understand more about what brought you to that abandoned building. Not the fire itself, but what you were seeking there."

I hesitated. This was dangerous territory, too close to the truths I couldn't tell. "Privacy," I said finally. "Control. Somewhere that was just mine."

She nodded as if this made perfect sense. "Those are things most people need, especially adolescents. How long had you been using that space?"

"A few months."

"And before you found that building, where did you go for privacy and control?"

I thought about my bedroom at my grandparents' apartment, where Papi could enter at any time. About school, where I was constantly observed. About the endless performance of normal family life where I played my assigned role.

"Nowhere," I admitted. "There wasn't anywhere."

Dr. Fontaine's expression softened slightly. "So losing that space in the fire, that was a significant loss for you."

Something tightened in my chest. No one had acknowledged that before, that beyond the legal trouble and property damage, I had lost something vital to my survival.

"Yes," I said, my voice smaller than I intended.

She nodded, not pushing further. "Our time is almost up for today. Before you go, I want to clarify what you can expect from me in these sessions."

I braced myself for platitudes about healing journeys and opening up.

"I won't lie to you, Sophie," she said instead. "If you ask me a direct question, I'll either answer it honestly or tell you why I can't. I won't pretend to know things I don't. I won't make promises I can't keep. And I will never pretend that therapy is a magic solution to complex problems."

I studied her face, looking for signs of deception. "That's a pretty low bar."

"Perhaps. But it's one I can guarantee I'll clear." She stood up. "For next week, I'd like you to think about what you need from these sessions. Not what the court needs or what your family expects, what you need."

As I left her office, I felt oddly off-balance. I'd come armed for battle, prepared to deflect and defend. Instead, Dr. Fontaine had acknowledged my weapons and then declined to fight.

I wasn't foolish enough to believe she was completely different from other adults. She still had her agenda, her professional obligations, her own hidden thoughts about the troubled teenager in her office.

But as I stepped into the Paris afternoon, I realized I was already thinking about what to tell her next week. And that, perhaps, was the most skillful manipulation of all.

Chapter 23. Let's go back to your childhood

"Let's go back to your childhood." Dr. Fontaine leaned forward slightly, her expression neutral but attentive. It was our third session, and she'd exhausted all her preliminary getting-to-know-you questions without extracting anything particularly revealing from me.

"Let's not," I replied, examining my fingernails. I'd chewed them to the quick the night before after overhearing Papi on the phone with Emilio again. Their conversation had been innocuous on the surface, something about a gallery opening in Berlin, but the casual way they spoke made my skin crawl.

"Why not?" Dr. Fontaine asked, resting her notepad on her knee. She never wrote much, just occasional words I couldn't decipher from my position. Today she wore a burgundy sweater that made her look less clinical, more human. I wasn't sure if that was deliberate.

"Because it's cliché," I said. "Daddy issues, Mommy issues, blah blah blah. Next you'll be showing me inkblots and asking what I see."

"I don't actually have inkblots," she responded with a slight smile. "Budget cuts."

Despite myself, I almost smiled back. Her understated humor was disarming, which was probably calculated, but still effective.

"Fine," I sighed, slumping deeper into the chair. "My childhood. What specifically do you want to know? When I stopped believing in Santa? The first time I got my period? My favorite breakfast cereal?"

"How about an early memory? Something that stands out to you, for whatever reason."

I considered deflecting again but decided to offer something harmless. The court needed to see progress, and Dr. Fontaine needed material for her reports. I could provide both without revealing anything truly important.

"I was about three," I began, choosing the shoe incident I'd mentioned in my first session with the court-appointed psychiatrist. It was safe territory, something I'd already processed. "My parents were getting ready to go out. Mom was dressed in this blue thing that floated when she moved. She always smelled like lemons back then."

I paused, surprised by the sensory detail that had emerged unbidden. I could almost smell it now, that sharp citrus scent that had once meant my mother was nearby.

"Go on," Dr. Fontaine encouraged when I fell silent.

"She was looking for a shoe and couldn't find it. My father was holding me, and we were watching her tear the bedroom apart. She found a shoe under the bed, but it wasn't the one she wanted, so she threw it across the room. It hit a lamp, which crashed and shattered glass everywhere."

"How did that make you feel?"

I rolled my eyes. "Really? That's the question you're going with?"

"It's a legitimate question, Sophie."

I stared out the window, where Paris rooftops created a gray geometry against a grayer sky. "I didn't feel anything. I was three. Kids that age don't analyze their parents' behavior; they just observe it."

"What did you observe, then?"

I turned back to her. "That my mother was dramatic. That when she wanted something, nothing else mattered, not safety, not common sense, not even me. I also observed that my father didn't do anything to stop her. He just held me and watched."

Dr. Fontaine nodded, making one of her cryptic notes. "You've developed quite an insight into that moment for someone who was only three."

"I've had time to think about it."

"Has your mother always been... dramatic, as you put it?"

I thought about Racine's cocaine binges, her theatrical displays of maternal concern for public consumption, her constant reinvention of herself. "Yeah, you could say that."

"What happened after the lamp broke?"

I hadn't expected her to return to the story. Most adults were content with whatever morsel I tossed them, eager to draw their own conclusions. Dr. Fontaine seemed genuinely interested in the full narrative.

"My father called the cleaning lady, who spent the rest of the day cleaning up glass. They never went out. Mom stayed on the couch drinking whiskey."

"And what did you do?"

"Played with my dolls. I liked to bandage them up, pretend they'd broken arms or legs jumping off things."

"Did you want to hurt the dolls?"

I shook my head, somewhat offended by the implication. "No, I wanted to help them. They were brave. They did dangerous things and got hurt, but then they healed. They weren't victims; they were adventurers."

Dr. Fontaine smiled, a genuine one this time. "That's a lovely perspective. You saw them as resilient, not damaged."

I hadn't thought of it that way before. "I guess so."

"Did you find the shoe your mother was looking for?"

I blinked, surprised again by her attention to detail. "Yes, actually. Later that evening, I found it buried in the bathroom trash basket. I brought it to her, all proud of myself for solving the mystery."

"How did she react?"

"She told me to put it back in the bathroom. Which I did." I paused, remembering something I hadn't considered before. "The weird thing is, I never told her where I found it. I just brought it to her. But she knew exactly where to tell me to return it."

"What does that suggest to you?"

I frowned, the implications only now becoming clear. "That she knew where it was all along. That the whole drama about the missing shoe was... what? A performance? An excuse for a tantrum? I don't know."

"Could it have been something else? A test, perhaps?"

The word sent a chill through me, reminding me of the Russian dolls, of Papi's manipulations.

"A test of what?" My voice came out sharper than I intended.

"I don't know, Sophie. That's what I'm asking you. In your understanding of your mother, what might she have been testing?"

I stared at the bland watercolor landscape on Dr. Fontaine's wall, letting my vision blur until the colors merged into meaningless smudges. What had Racine been testing? My father's patience? His willingness to clean up her messes, literal and figurative? Or had she been testing me, even then, to see how I would respond to chaos?

"Maybe she was testing whether we would still love her even when she was being horrible," I said finally, the words emerging from some deep place I rarely accessed. "Whether she could get away with destroying things and still be... wanted."

Dr. Fontaine's expression remained neutral, but her eyes softened slightly. "That's a very insightful observation."

"Or maybe she was just drunk and lost her shoe." I shrugged, uncomfortable with the vulnerability I'd just displayed. "People don't always have deep motivations for being assholes."

"True. But you mentioned your mother was an alcoholic. In your experience, was her drinking random, or did it follow patterns?"

I thought about the countless evenings Racine had spent with a tumbler of whiskey, the good crystal one she reserved for herself. How those evenings often followed social rejections, professional disappointments, or arguments with my father.

"There were patterns," I admitted. "She drank when she felt inadequate or unwanted. Or when my father paid attention to someone else."

"Someone like you?"

The question landed like a slap. I'd never considered that my mother might have been jealous of the relationship I had with my father, limited though it was.

"I was just a kid," I said.

"Children can still be perceived as rivals for attention, especially by someone with..." she hesitated, choosing her words carefully, "certain insecurities."

I remembered the shoe incident again, how my father had been holding me when my mother's meltdown began. How her eyes had darted between us before she started her frantic search. Had that been the trigger? His attention directed at me instead of her?

"I found another shoe memory," I said, surprising myself. "I was maybe five or six. We were going to a restaurant, someplace fancy. My mother spent an hour getting ready, and when she came out of the bedroom, my father said, 'You look beautiful.' Then he looked at me in my little dress and said, 'So does Sophie.' My mother went back into the bedroom and changed her entire outfit. We were an hour late to dinner."

Dr. Fontaine nodded, her expression thoughtful. "What do you make of that?"

"That she couldn't stand sharing the spotlight, even with a kindergartner."

"And how did you adapt to that reality? What strategies did you develop?"

I picked at a loose thread on my sleeve, considering the question. "I learned to be invisible when it served me, and to perform when that served me. I became whatever each situation required."

"That's a heavy burden for a child."

"It's survival," I corrected her. "You do what you have to do."

She studied me for a moment, her gaze neither pitying nor judgmental. "Sophie, in our first session, you mentioned that all adults lie. I'm curious about the lies in your childhood. Were they overt falsehoods, or more subtle distortions?"

I thought about the shoe in the trash, about my mother's theatrical search. About the countless times Racine had promised to attend school events then "forgotten." About my father's assurances that he'd call every Sunday after the divorce, calls that rarely came.

"Both," I said. "Some were direct lies, like my mother saying she'd be somewhere when she had no intention of showing up. Others were... I don't know, lies of omission? Things no one talked about, like her drinking or my father's 'lovers.' Reality just got bent until it fit whatever narrative they needed in the moment."

"And where did you fit in these bent realities?"

I laughed, a short, harsh sound. "I was a prop. Sometimes the perfect daughter they could show off, sometimes the burden they had to manage, sometimes just... background."

"That sounds lonely," Dr. Fontaine said quietly.

Something twisted in my chest, a sharp ache I hadn't allowed myself to feel in years. I swallowed hard against the sudden pressure in my throat.

"It was what it was," I said, my voice rougher than usual. "You adapt."

"Yes, you do. Children are remarkably adaptable." She set her notepad aside. "But adaptation doesn't come without cost. Those early patterns, those survival strategies, they become part of us. They shape how we see the world, how we respond to threat, real or perceived."

I thought about the abandoned building, my wall of words turned to ash. About the vodka I still relied on to numb the constant vigilance that exhausted me. About my reflexive distrust of anyone who offered help or claimed to care.

"So what's your professional assessment, Dr. Fontaine?" I asked, aiming for sarcasm but landing closer to genuine inquiry than I'd intended. "Am I irreparably damaged by my dysfunctional childhood?"

"No, Sophie, I don't believe you are." Her voice was firm, certain. "Shaped by it, yes. Carrying wounds from it, certainly. But 'irreparably damaged'? Not even close."

"How can you be so sure?"

"Because you're here, telling me these stories. Because despite everything you've experienced, you're still searching for truth, still capable of insight, still able to recognize when something isn't right. Those are not the qualities of someone irreparably damaged."

I looked away, uncomfortable with her assessment, with the faint flicker of hope it ignited. Hope was dangerous. Hope made you vulnerable.

"Time's almost up," I said, glancing at the clock.

Dr. Fontaine nodded, accepting my retreat. "Before you go, I want to thank you for sharing those memories. I know it wasn't easy."

I stood, gathering my bag. "It was just the shoe story. Nothing earth-shattering."

"Sometimes the seemingly small moments reveal the most." She rose as well. "For next time, would you consider thinking about friendships in your childhood? Any connections that were important to you?"

I thought about Ruby, our blood sisterhood, the Oreos dunked in whiskey, the kiss that ended everything. "Maybe," I said noncommittally.

As I left Dr. Fontaine's office, I realized I'd given her more than I'd intended to, revealed patterns I hadn't fully acknowledged even to myself. It was unsettling, this gradual unspooling of defenses I'd maintained for years.

But as I stepped onto the Paris street, breathing in the cold December air, I also felt something unexpected: a slight lightening, as if sharing those childhood memories had reduced their weight by some small degree. Not enough to change anything fundamental, but enough to notice.

Just enough to make me wonder what else might change if I kept talking.

Chapter 24. Would you like to draw something today?

Dr. Fontaine greeted me with her usual calm smile when I arrived for our fourth session. I was three minutes early, a fact I didn't acknowledge and hoped she wouldn't either. My punctuality wasn't a sign of enthusiasm for therapy, my other option had been lingering at the apartment where Papi was hosting one of his "cultural salons," which basically meant a bunch of men drinking expensive cognac while pontificating about art they didn't understand.

"You look tired," she said as I dropped into my usual chair.

"Is that your professional assessment?" I asked, but the sarcasm felt mechanical. I was tired. I'd been having nightmares again, fragmented scenes where I was running through endless hotel corridors while men in dark suits followed at a measured pace, never rushing but somehow always gaining on me.

"Just an observation." She settled into her chair, wearing a deep blue sweater today that made her look less clinical. "How has your week been?"

I shrugged. "The usual. School, homework, pretending my family isn't completely twisted."

"And how is that going? The pretending?"

"I deserve an Oscar." I picked at a thread on my sleeve. "My mother has decided she's going to be stable for a while. She's been sober for almost two weeks, a new record. She's redecorating her apartment and talking about having me move in with her."

Dr. Fontaine's expression remained neutral, but I saw the slight tension around her eyes. "How do you feel about that possibility?"

"Depends which day you ask me. Sometimes living with an unreliable addict seems like an upgrade from living with..." I stopped, catching myself before saying too much about Papi.

"From your current situation," Dr. Fontaine finished diplomatically.

"Right."

She studied me for a moment, then reached for a drawer in her desk. "Would you like to try something different today?"

I tensed. "Different how?"

"Nothing dramatic." She pulled out a sketchpad and a small box of colored pencils. "I thought we might work with some visual expression."

"You want me to draw?" I looked at the art supplies with suspicion. "Is this where you analyze my psyche based on whether I use red or blue?"

"No color-based psychological profiling, I promise." A subtle smile crossed her face. "Sometimes it's just easier to express certain things visually than verbally."

"I'm not an artist."

"That's perfect. I'm not looking for technical skill."

I eyed the sketchpad. "What would I draw?"

"Whatever comes to mind. Or perhaps something specific, like a dream you've had recently."

Something shifted in my chest. Dreams. I'd mentioned a dream briefly in our first session, the cat under the bed. I hadn't intended to bring it up again, but it had continued to haunt me, expanding and evolving in new iterations almost nightly.

"I don't remember my dreams," I lied.

Dr. Fontaine didn't challenge me directly. "It doesn't have to be a recent dream. Any dream that's stayed with you would work."

I took the pad and pencils, mostly to end the conversation. The paper was blank and slightly textured under my fingers. I selected a pencil at random, blue, as it happened, and held it poised above the page.

"What if I just sit here not drawing anything?" I asked.

"That's your choice," she said simply. "We can sit in silence for the next forty minutes if you prefer."

The thought of forty minutes of silence was somehow more uncomfortable than drawing. I sighed dramatically and began sketching a rough rectangle.

"Fine. I'll draw that dream I mentioned before. The one with the bedroom."

"Take your time," Dr. Fontaine said. She picked up her notepad but didn't write anything, just waited.

I started with the basic elements: the four walls of the bedroom, a window on one wall, a bed in the center of the room. My drawing was rudimentary, the perspective slightly off, but the basic elements were recognizable. I hesitated before adding the cat, just its face peering out from beneath the bed, whiskers extending outward like sensory probes.

"It's not very good," I said, turning the pad toward her.

Dr. Fontaine studied it without judgment. "Tell me about what you've drawn."

"It's the dream I mentioned before. There's a bedroom with a window and a bed. Under the bed, there's a cat. That's it."

"What else do you notice about the room?"

I looked at my drawing again. "There's no door. I didn't realize that until I drew it."

"A room with no door, just a window," she observed. "What might that suggest?"

I shrugged. "Bad architectural planning?"

"Or?"

I stared at my crude drawing. The windowsill was the only interruption in the continuous lines of walls. "The only way in or out is through the window."

"And windows are typically..."

"Higher up. Less accessible." I frowned. "Is this where you tell me the room represents my mind or something equally cliché?"

"I'm not going to tell you what your dream means, Sophie. I'm curious about what you think it means."

I tapped the pencil against the paper. "If the room is... me, I guess, then the window is how I let things in or out. But it's high up, so I can control it. Nobody gets in without me allowing it."

Dr. Fontaine nodded encouragingly. "And the bed?"

"It's where you're supposed to rest, to feel safe." I glanced down at my drawing. I'd made the bed neatly made, the blanket crisply rendered in blue. "But nobody's in the bed."

"Why do you think that is?"

"I don't know. Maybe it's not safe enough."

"What about the cat?"

I stared at the feline face I'd drawn, hiding under the bed. Its eyes were wide, alert, watching. "The cat is hiding."

"From what?"

"I don't know," I said, irritation creeping into my voice. "It's just a cat under a bed."

"You mentioned before that the cat was the most significant part of the dream," Dr. Fontaine reminded me gently. "You called it the 'money shot.'"

I had said that, in our first session. Why had the cat felt so important? I looked at its face again, the whiskers I'd drawn exaggerated and curled.

"The cat looks scared," I said finally. "It's hiding under the bed even though there's no one else in the room."

"What might it be afraid of?"

I closed my eyes, trying to recall the dream more fully. In the original version, the cat had just been a face beneath the bed. But in recent iterations, there was something else...

"In the dream, there's something the cat can sense that I can't see," I said slowly. "Something outside the window, watching. Waiting to get in."

Dr. Fontaine was quiet, letting me continue without interruption.

"The cat knows it's there, but I don't. That's why the cat is hiding." I opened my eyes, looking at my drawing with new understanding. "The cat is trying to warn me, but also trying to protect itself."

"If the room represents you," Dr. Fontaine said carefully, "and the cat is hiding within that room, what might the cat represent?"

The answer came with surprising clarity. "The part of me that knows the truth. The part that sees danger even when I pretend everything is normal."

I reached for the black pencil and began shading the outside of the window, making it dark, ominous. "There's always something waiting to get in. Always someone watching, looking for a way past my defenses." My hand was moving almost on its own now, the black getting darker, more insistent around the edges of the window.

"And the cat hides because..."

"Because it's not strong enough to fight what's out there. It can only hide and hope not to be found." My voice had dropped to almost a whisper. "It's just a small cat. What could it do against... against them?"

Dr. Fontaine leaned forward slightly. "Against who, Sophie?"

I froze, pencil pressed so hard against the paper it was in danger of tearing. I'd said too much. Let my guard down, let something real slip through.

"No one specific," I said, backtracking. "Just... general bad things. Danger. Whatever."

Dr. Fontaine didn't press directly. Instead, she asked, "If you could add something to this room to make the cat feel safer, what would it be?"

I considered the question. "A door with a lock. A really strong one."

"What else?"

"Maybe another cat. So it's not alone." I surprised myself with this answer.

"That's a good thought," Dr. Fontaine said. "Would you like to add those things to your drawing?"

I hesitated, then picked up the pencil again. I added a sturdy door to one wall, complete with an oversized lock. Then, after a moment's thought, I drew another cat face beside the first one under the bed.

"They're still hiding," Dr. Fontaine observed.

"They're safer together, but the danger is still out there." I set the pencil down. "This is stupid. It's just a drawing."

"Art has a way of expressing things we might not say directly," Dr. Fontaine said. "What do you think this drawing is expressing?"

I stared at the image I'd created: the locked room, the dark window, the two cats hiding together under an empty bed. "That I'm afraid. That part of me is always hiding, always watching for danger. That I don't really rest, even when I should feel safe."

My throat tightened unexpectedly. These weren't new revelations, I'd known I was afraid, known I was constantly vigilant, but seeing it rendered so plainly on paper made it more real somehow. More undeniable.

"The cats are small, but they're smart," Dr. Fontaine said gently. "They know when to hide, when to seek safety in numbers. That's survival intelligence, Sophie."

"But they're still just hiding. They're not fixing anything."

"Sometimes hiding is how we survive until we can do more." She tilted her head slightly. "What would the cats need to come out from under the bed?"

I thought about this. "They'd need to know the window was secure. That nothing could get in without their permission."

"And what would that look like in your life?"

The question hung in the air between us. What would it look like to feel secure? To know that Papi and men like him couldn't reach me?

But looking at her, fragile, defensive, wrapped in layers of denial built over decades, I know she's not ready to hear it. May never be ready.

For the first time in my life, I see Mamie, really see her. Not as the elegant, remote grandmother who judges my clothes and posture, but as a woman who was once a girl. A girl who learned to survive by pretending not to see, who passed that blindness to her daughter, who might have passed it to me if I hadn't found my way into this room.

"I'm sorry, Sophie," Mamie whispers, a tear spilling down her carefully made-up cheek. "For whatever you've been through that I didn't... that I couldn't..."

She can't finish the sentence. She doesn't know what she's apologizing for, not really. But it's the first time she's acknowledged that my reality might be different from the carefully constructed one she inhabits.

It's not enough. It's nowhere near enough. But looking at Dr. Fontaine's encouraging expression, at Mamie's genuine, if limited, distress, I realize it might be a beginning.

"Thank you for coming today," I say, my voice steadier than I feel. "I think... I think maybe we understand each other a little better now."

As Mamie dabs carefully at her eyes, I catch Dr. Fontaine's gaze. The slight nod she gives me feels like recognition of something important. Not a victory, exactly, but a step away from the silence that has defined my family for generations.

Chapter 28. How do you feel about seeing your mother today?

Dr. Fontaine's question hangs in the air between us like cigarette smoke in a closed room. I've been dreading this moment since she proposed the joint session last week.

"Thrilled," I say, examining my chewed fingernails. "Who wouldn't be excited to see their mother fresh out of rehab? Do you think she'll bring her ninety-day sobriety chip for show and tell?"

Dr. Fontaine's expression doesn't change, but her eyes soften slightly. "It's natural to feel apprehensive, Sophie. This will be your first structured conversation with your mother since she entered treatment."

I laugh, a sharp sound that bounces off the walls. "What am I hoping for? World peace? A pony? A mother who suddenly develops the ability to see beyond her own reflection?"

"Those sound like things you don't believe are possible," she observes. "I'm asking what you genuinely hope might happen, even if it's something small."

I stare at the dream drawing still tacked to her corkboard, the room with the window, the cats hiding under the bed. What do I hope for? The question feels dangerous, like stepping onto thin ice.

"I hope she stays sober," I say finally. "Not for me. For her. She's less... destructive when she's sober."

Dr. Fontaine nods. "That's a compassionate hope, Sophie."

"It's self-preservation," I correct her. "Her chaos affects me."

"Both things can be true."

The sound of heels clicking on hardwood drifts in from the waiting room. My shoulders tense automatically. Dr. Fontaine glances at her watch.

"She's early," I say, surprised. Racine is chronically, almost pathologically late for everything.

"Another sign she's taking this seriously," Dr. Fontaine suggests.

Before I can respond, there's a soft knock. Dr. Fontaine rises and opens the door, and suddenly the room seems too small, too airless. Racine stands in the doorway wearing a simple gray dress I've never seen before. Her hair is pulled back in a low ponytail, her face notably free of makeup except for a touch of mascara. No dramatic entrance, no cloud of expensive perfume.

"Hello, Sophie," she says, her voice missing its usual performative lilt.

I say nothing, studying her like she's a specimen under glass. Without her armor of designer clothes and perfect makeup, she looks older, more fragile. Her eyes, startlingly like mine, meet my gaze briefly before looking away.

"Please, have a seat, Ms. Jamison," Dr. Fontaine says, gesturing to the chair next to mine. I notice she's arranged them differently today, a slight angle, close enough for conversation but far enough to maintain psychological space.

Racine sits carefully, her movements measured. Her hands rest in her lap, and I notice her nails are short, unpolished. Another shock. My mother's manicures were sacred rituals, never missed even during her worst binges.

"Thank you for agreeing to this session," she says to me. "My counselor at Clairmont thought it would be beneficial, but I told her it was entirely up to you."

Clairmont. The fancy rehab center north of Paris where celebrities dry out between film projects. Of course Racine wouldn't choose a standard treatment facility. Even in recovery, certain standards must be maintained.

"How magnanimous of you," I say, unable to keep the edge from my voice.

Dr. Fontaine interjects gently. "Perhaps we could begin with Racine sharing a bit about her experience in treatment, if she's comfortable doing so."

My mother nods, folding and unfolding her hands. A nervous gesture I've never seen before. The Racine I know is never visibly nervous; she channels anxiety into dramatic flourishes.

"I've been at Clairmont for six weeks," she begins. "It wasn't my first attempt at getting sober, as Sophie knows, but it's the first time I've... stayed. Completed the program." She glances at me. "It's also the first time I went voluntarily, not because of a crisis or external pressure."

"What changed?" Dr. Fontaine asks.

Racine takes a deep breath. "After our argument, Sophie, when you found me in that abandoned building, after you walked away... I couldn't stop thinking about what you said. About me knowing what my father did to you, what he'd done to me, and still leaving you with him."

I feel my pulse quicken. She's acknowledging it. Out loud. In front of a witness.

"You were right," she continues. "I knew... something. Not specifics, not until that day, but I knew he wasn't safe. Deep down, I always knew." Her voice catches. "And I left you there anyway."

The room seems to tilt slightly. I've spent so long bracing against denial that her admission leaves me unbalanced.

"Why?" The word escapes before I can stop it, small and sharp.

"Because acknowledging it meant facing what happened to me," she says, her eyes fixed on her hands. "And I couldn't. Not without something to numb me." She looks up. "That's not an excuse. It's just the truth. I chose my addiction over your safety because facing reality sober was unthinkable."

Dr. Fontaine watches us carefully, allowing the moment to unfold without intervention.

"What happened to you?" I ask, half-dreading the answer.

Racine's eyes close briefly. "It started when I was twelve. After a ballet recital. One of his friends complimented my performance, said I showed 'remarkable maturity and grace.' My father suggested I might want to show his friend some of my practice routines in private." She swallows hard. "It progressed from there. Different friends. Different 'private performances.'"

Bile rises in my throat. "Mamie didn't know?"

"She suspected, I think. But she did what she'd learned to do, looked away, pretended not to see. Just like I did with you." Racine's hands tremble slightly. "At Clairmont, they call it generational trauma. The patterns we pass down because we don't know how to break them."

I think of the Russian dolls, nested inside each other, passing their shapes from one generation to the next. "Is that why you took me to America? To get away from him?"

She nods. "I thought distance would protect us both. I thought an ocean would be enough." A bitter smile crosses her face. "But I brought all my damage with me, didn't I? The drinking, the men, the need for constant attention and validation. I just recreated a different version of the same dysfunction."

"And then you brought me back to him," I say, unable to keep the accusation from my voice.

"I told myself he wouldn't dare. That you were his granddaughter, not just some ballet student. That it had been years, that he'd changed." Her voice hardens. "I needed to believe that because the alternative, that I was delivering you back to a predator, was unthinkable."

"Until Nice," I say quietly.

Racine's face pales. "Yes. Until Nice."

Dr. Fontaine leans forward slightly. "Sophie, would you be comfortable explaining what happened in Nice, for context?"

I hesitate, feeling the familiar tightness in my chest whenever I think about those days. "My grandfather took me to Nice, supposedly as a Christmas gift. He had arranged to meet two friends there, Emilio and Alejandro. It became clear they had... expectations. Plans involving me."

"What kind of plans?" Dr. Fontaine asks, her voice carefully neutral.

"They were going to..." I falter, the words sticking in my throat.

"They were going to rape my daughter," Racine finishes, her voice flat but firm. "My father and his pedophile friends planned to rape my fourteen-year-old daughter, just as they had raped other girls over the years, including me."

The bluntness of her statement hits like a physical blow. No euphemisms, no softening, just the stark, ugly truth spoken aloud for the first time.

"I got away," I say, my voice steadier than I feel. "A woman helped me, Isabelle, Alejandro's wife. And Raul, a friend of Papi's who had been watching him, suspecting what he was capable of. They helped me escape and get back to Paris."

Dr. Fontaine nods, taking this in. "And afterward?"

"Afterward, my grandfather convinced my grandmother it was all a misunderstanding about a nude beach." I laugh, a hollow sound. "And my mother was conveniently absent, as usual."

"I was using," Racine admits. "Heavily. When I returned and you tried to tell me what happened, I... God, Sophie, I'm so sorry." Her eyes fill with tears. "I knew you were telling the truth. I knew because the same men had done the same things to me. But acknowledging it meant facing my own trauma, and I couldn't. Not then."

"And now?" I ask, hating the flicker of hope that rises in me.

"Now I'm trying to face everything, without numbing myself. It's excruciating." She smiles sadly. "They say recovery is about making amends, but some things can't be made right with words or gestures. Some betrayals are too fundamental."

"So why are you here?" I ask. "If you don't think I can forgive you?"

"I'm not asking for forgiveness, Sophie. I don't deserve it." She meets my eyes directly. "I'm here because you deserve the truth. You deserve a mother who can face reality without dissolving or disappearing. I can't change what happened, but I can try to be present now, even when it's painful. Especially when it's painful."

The room falls quiet. Outside, Paris traffic creates a distant hum. Rain begins to patter against the window, soft and insistent.

"I've moved to a new apartment," Racine says eventually. "Nothing fancy. One bedroom for me, one for you, if you ever wanted to visit. No pressure." She hesitates. "I've reported my father to the authorities. Provided names, dates, details I'd suppressed for years. My therapist is helping me through the process."

I stare at her, stunned. "You reported Papi? To the police?"

She nods. "It may not lead to prosecution. These men are connected, protected. But there will be a record. An official account. My truth alongside yours."

"Mamie must be, "

"Devastated. Furious. In denial." Racine sighs. "We're not speaking. She says I'm making it up for attention, that I've always been 'creatively dramatic.' But I expected that."

Dr. Fontaine checks the clock. "We have about fifteen minutes left. Sophie, is there anything specific you'd like to ask your mother during our remaining time?"

I have a thousand questions, but one rises to the surface. "Are you really going to stay sober this time?" I hate how young my voice sounds, how the question reveals the child still hoping against hope.

"I want to say yes," Racine answers carefully. "But one thing recovery has taught me is not to make promises I might not keep. I can tell you I'm committed to sobriety today. I can tell you I have a program, a sponsor, ongoing therapy. I can tell you I'm fighting for it harder than I've ever fought for anything." She pauses. "But I won't promise you it will stick forever. That would be another lie, and you've had enough of those."

Her honesty catches me off guard. The mother I knew would have made grand declarations, sworn on everything holy, perhaps even cried theatrical tears to emphasize her sincerity. This measured response, this refusal to promise the unpromisable, feels new. More authentic.

"Thank you," I say quietly. "For not lying."

Racine nods, a ghost of a smile crossing her face. "Baby steps, right?"

As the session ends and we prepare to leave, an awkward moment hangs between us in the waiting room. The old Racine would have pulled me into a perfumed embrace, regardless of my comfort. This new version stands an arm's length away, uncertain.

"Would it be alright if I called you?" she asks. "Maybe once a week? Just to check in?"

I consider this. "Texts might be better. To start."

She nods, accepting the boundary without protest. "Texts it is." She hesitates. "Take care of yourself, Sophie."

As I watch her walk away, her shoulders straighter than they've been in years despite the burden she's carrying, I feel something shift inside me. Not forgiveness, not yet. Not trust, certainly. But perhaps the beginning of something else, a cautious recognition that people might change, even after a lifetime of disappointing you.

It's a dangerous thought. A hopeful one. I'm not sure which scares me more.

Chapter 29. Your father sent this letter

The letter arrived on a Tuesday, an innocuous white envelope with American stamps and my father's careful handwriting. Mamie handed it to me at breakfast, her face carefully neutral. Since Racine had reported Papi to the authorities two weeks ago, the apartment had become a minefield of unspoken tensions. Mamie and I circled each other like wary cats, maintaining the facade of normalcy while avoiding any topic of substance.

"From your father," she said unnecessarily, setting it beside my orange juice.

I stared at the envelope, my appetite evaporating. "Thanks."

She hovered for a moment, perhaps expecting me to open it immediately, to share its contents with her. When I slipped it into my school bag untouched, a flicker of disappointment crossed her face.

"Will you write back?" she asked, trying to sound casual.

"I don't know what it says yet," I pointed out, taking a deliberate bite of toast.

"Of course," she murmured, retreating to the kitchen.

I waited until I was alone in the Metro, crushed between morning commuters, before extracting the letter. Four pages of my father's distinctive handwriting, spiky and compressed, as if he were trying to fit too many thoughts into too little space. I refolded them without reading and tucked them back into my bag.

Whatever he had to say could wait until I was ready to hear it.

By the time I arrived at Dr. Fontaine's office at 3:57 pm, the letter had become a live thing in my bag, radiating heat and demand. I'd carried it through seven academic classes without once looking at it, a feat of willpower that left me exhausted.

"You seem distracted today," Dr. Fontaine observed as I took my seat.

I pulled the envelope from my bag and placed it on the small table between us. "My father sent this."

Dr. Fontaine glanced at the letter but didn't reach for it. "Have you read it?"

"Not yet."

"What's stopping you?"

I stared at the ceiling, gathering my thoughts. "Once I read it, I have to deal with it. With him. With how I feel about him."

"And how do you feel about him?" she asked gently.

"I don't know," I admitted. "He's basically a stranger at this point. We haven't seen each other since the divorce. He calls maybe three times a year, always on speakerphone so whoever he's dating can hear what a devoted father he is."

"That sounds painful."

I shrugged. "It's just reality. He didn't fight for custody. He didn't object when my mother moved me to France. He became a Buddhist and focused on his 'inner journey' or whatever."

"Yet you kept the letter," Dr. Fontaine noted. "You brought it here."

"Where else would I take it?" I gestured around her office. "This is apparently where I process all my family dysfunction now."

She smiled slightly at that. "Would you like to read it now? We could discuss it afterward."

I hesitated, then picked up the envelope. "Not out loud."

"Of course not. Take whatever time you need."

I slid my finger under the flap and extracted the pages. The paper was thick, expensive, with a watermark visible when held to the light. Classic Dad, making even an apology feel like a luxury item.

"Your mother wrote to me last week. She explained that she's recently completed treatment and is working to make amends for past failures. She suggested I might consider doing the same.

At first, I was defensive. I told myself I'd always provided financially, that I'd respected your mother's parental decisions, that I'd maintained some contact despite the distance. Dr. Fontaine's expression remained gentle. "What are your thoughts after reading it?"

"It's very... Buddhist," I said, aiming for sarcasm but landing somewhere closer to pain. "Lots of talk about self-reflection and enlightenment. He even manages to make abandoning your child sound spiritually evolved."

I considered this. "He's not wrong about the facts. He didn't fight for custody. He didn't stay involved in any meaningful way." I picked at a loose thread on my sleeve. "And I guess admitting that counts for something."

The question caught me off guard. What did I want from him? An apology couldn't change the past. Promises couldn't be trusted. Explanations couldn't fill the father-shaped hole in my life.

"I don't know," I said finally. "Maybe that's the problem. I don't know what I want from him because I don't know him. And he definitely doesn't know me."

"That's a very insightful observation," Dr. Fontaine said. "Relationships require mutual knowledge, especially parent-child relationships."

"Well, we don't have that. He says he'd 'welcome hearing from me,' but what would I even say? 'Hey Dad, guess what, your ex-wife left me with her pedophile father, but don't worry, I'm getting excellent therapy now'?"

I laughed, a short, sharp sound. "God, no. I don't want to tell him anything. He lost the right to know me when he let Mom take me to France without a fight."

"And yet, you kept the letter. You brought it here."

I fell silent, turning her observation over in my mind. Why had I kept it? Why not tear it up unread, or read it and discard it? Why bring it to therapy?

"I guess... part of me still wants a father," I admitted, the words feeling like stones in my mouth. "How pathetic is that?"

"It's not pathetic at all," Dr. Fontaine said firmly. "The desire for parental connection is one of our most basic human needs. The fact that you can acknowledge that desire, despite repeated disappointment, speaks to your emotional resilience."

"Or my stupidity," I muttered.

"Hoping for connection isn't stupid, Sophie. It's brave."

I stared at the letter in my lap, thinking about bravery. Was it brave to keep trying? Or was it braver to walk away, to stop investing in people who had proven themselves unworthy of trust?

"What do you think I should do?" I asked. "Write back? Ignore him? Send a glitter bomb?"

Dr. Fontaine smiled slightly at the last suggestion. "I can't make that decision for you. But I can help you think through what each option might mean for you."

"If I write back, I'm opening myself up to more disappointment," I said, thinking aloud. "If I ignore him, I'm basically doing to him what he did to me. And glitter bombs are apparently an environmental hazard."

"What would writing back on your own terms look like?" Dr. Fontaine asked. "Not giving him everything, but perhaps starting a conversation that serves your needs?"

I considered this. "I could acknowledge the letter without committing to a relationship. Set some boundaries." I bit my lip. "I'm not ready to talk about what happened with Papi. I'm not even sure he deserves to know."

"You get to decide what you share and when," Dr. Fontaine confirmed. "Engaging doesn't mean surrendering your autonomy."

I nodded slowly. "Maybe I could write a short email. Nothing emotional. Just... an acknowledgment."

"That sounds like a measured approach."

"But what if he takes it as an invitation to waltz back into my life like nothing happened?" I asked, anxiety rising. "What if he suddenly decides to play concerned father now that the hard years are over?"

"Then you'll set boundaries," Dr. Fontaine said simply. "You'll decide what kind of relationship you're willing to have, if any. The power is yours, Sophie."

The power is yours. The words resonated in ways I hadn't expected. For so long, I'd felt powerless, a child shuffled between divorced parents, moved across an ocean, left with predatory grandparents, ignored when I tried to speak the truth. But I wasn't that child anymore. I could choose my level of engagement. I could protect myself without cutting off all possibility.

"I think I'll write back," I said finally. "Just a short note. Letting him know I received the letter. Maybe asking a few questions to see if he's actually interested in knowing me, not just relieving his guilt."

Dr. Fontaine nodded. "Would you like to draft that here, or take some time to think about it?"

"I'll think about it first," I decided. "I don't want to react from emotions. I want to be... strategic."

"Strategic?"

"Careful," I amended. "With myself, not just with him. I don't want to give more than I can afford to lose."

As I left Dr. Fontaine's office and stepped into the Paris afternoon, the letter felt lighter in my bag. Not because its contents had changed, but because my relationship to it had. It wasn't a bomb that might detonate at any moment. It was just a letter from a flawed man who'd made selfish choices. I could acknowledge it without being defined by it.

Later that night, I opened my laptop and typed a short email to the address my father had provided:

"Dad, I received your letter today. Thank you for acknowledging the impact of your choices. I'm not ready for phone calls or visits, but I'm open to limited communication through email.

If you're interested in knowing who I am now, rather than who I was at ten, you could start by asking questions about my actual life instead of making assumptions. I have questions too, but they'll wait until I see whether this exchange is genuine.

Sophie"

My finger hovered over the send button for a full minute before I finally clicked, releasing the message into the digital ether. I didn't know if anything would come of it. I didn't know if he'd respect my boundaries or disappoint me again. But for the first time, I wasn't waiting for him to determine my worth. I was setting the terms of engagement.

It felt, unexpectedly, like freedom.

Chapter 30: Where do you want to live, Sophie?

Dr. Fontaine's waiting room hadn't changed in the five months I'd been coming here. Same lavender scent, same mediocre watercolors, same slightly uncomfortable chairs. My probation officer had already indicated that my hundred hours of community service, mostly sorting donations at a women's shelter, had been "exemplary." When Dr. Fontaine opened her door precisely at 4:00 pm, I felt an unexpected pang. This routine, annoying as it had sometimes been, had become a strange anchor.

She smiled. "Therapists are human too, Sophie. And yes, I feel genuine pride in the work you've done here."

"Which brings us to an important question," she said, redirecting seamlessly. "Where do you want to live, Sophie?"

"Option two: move in with a newly sober alcoholic who's one bad day away from relapse. Become her emotional support child while she processes decades of trauma. Hard pass."

"Your father has mentioned the possibility of you visiting America, perhaps for an extended stay," Dr. Fontaine noted.

I laughed. "Option three: move across an ocean to live with a practical stranger who suddenly remembered he has a daughter. Start over in a country I barely remember with a man who's spent more time meditating than parenting. What could possibly go wrong?"

The question caught me off guard. What did I need? Not just what I wanted to avoid, but what I actually needed to move forward?

"I need safety," I said finally, surprising myself with the raw honesty in my voice. "Not just physical safety. I need to feel like I can breathe, like I'm not constantly waiting for the next disaster. Like I'm not responsible for managing everyone else's damage while trying to fix my own."

"I need some kind of stability. Not perfect, I know that doesn't exist. But enough structure that I'm not constantly bracing for everything to collapse." I swallowed hard. "And I need... space. To figure out who I am when I'm not in crisis mode. When I'm not just reacting to whatever fresh hell my family has created."

"None of them, entirely," I admitted. "But... maybe my mother's apartment, with conditions."

"I keep seeing you, for starters," I said, the words tumbling out before I could censor them. "Not because a court says I have to, but because... it helps. Having someone who isn't caught in the family dysfunction, who can see things clearly."

"I'm not doing it for her," I said quickly. "I'm not moving in to support her recovery or whatever."

"I understand. You're doing it for yourself, because it offers the best combination of safety, stability, and space for your own growth." She made a final note, then set her pad aside. "It's a mature decision, Sophie. One that reflects genuine agency and self-awareness."

"Plans can change," Dr. Fontaine agreed. "But the skills you've developed, your ability to assess situations clearly, to advocate for your needs, to set boundaries, those will serve you regardless of where you live or what challenges arise."

The clock on her wall showed 4:47 pm. Nearly the end of our final court-mandated session. A wave of something like panic washed over me.

"So that's it?" I asked, my voice smaller than I intended. "I just... walk out of here and figure everything out on my own now?"

Dr. Fontaine leaned forward slightly. "Not at all. As you mentioned, our work together can continue if you choose. The court's requirements have been fulfilled, but that doesn't mean you've completed your healing journey."

"Healing journey," I repeated with a weak attempt at my usual sarcasm. "Very therapy-speak of you."

She smiled. "We all have our professional jargon. The point is, Sophie, this isn't an ending unless you want it to be."

Relief flooded through me, embarrassing in its intensity. "Okay. Good. Because I still have, like, a million issues to work through. The cats in my dream drawing are still hiding under the bed most of the time."

"But they have each other now," Dr. Fontaine pointed out. "And they have a door with a lock. Progress, not perfection, remember?"

As I gathered my things to leave, I paused by the door, struggling with words I wasn't used to saying. "Dr. Fontaine? Thank you. For... you know. Listening. Not lying to me. All of it."

She nodded, her eyes warm. "It's been my privilege, Sophie. I'll see you next week, same time?"

"Same time," I confirmed, stepping into the waiting room where an anxious-looking teenage boy had already taken my usual chair. Our eyes met briefly, his quickly darting away. I recognized that look, the suspicious wariness of someone who expected only disappointment from adults.

"It gets better," I found myself saying to him. "Not perfect. But better."

He looked up, startled, as if no one had ever suggested such a thing to him before.

I stepped out into the Paris afternoon, tilting my face up to catch the rare February sunshine. Around me, the city continued its eternal bustle, indifferent to my small victory. But for the first time since my grandfather had taken me to Nice, I felt something like possibility unfurling in my chest. Not happiness exactly, not yet, but the space where happiness might eventually grow.

It wasn't much. But it was mine. And for now, that was enough.

Chapter 31. So how has the past year been?
